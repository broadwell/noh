Form
Second Act - Shite Performs:
Kuse → Jonomai
Synopsis
The Yūgao spirit’s narration begins the recitation of waka poem sent to her by Genji, brigning her deeply into memories of the time when she was with him. The jonomai as a pure dance, does not engage with the story. However, in the given context, it represents a climax for the play by providing a vehicle for the actor and audience to project into the movement, dimensions of the play related to the yūgao flower, Lady Yūgao and the emotions of the romantic affair with Genji.
Text
Poetry in regular rhythm.
Music
In the short first section jo, the jiutai sings an einori chant, accompanied by percussion in flexible rhythmic setting, and nohkan's non-congruent. For the rest of the shōdan, the percussion is in strict rhythmic setting, paired with a congruent nohkan part.
Visuals
The jonomai is focused on dance with the rich choreography of kata, which make use of almost the entire stage-space.

At the end of the kuse, the spirit of Lady Yūgao starts to recite the very first poem that Prince Genji wrote to her, but as if poetic words could not fully express her deepest feelings anymore, taken by emotions, she interrupts her recitation, and begins to dance.

The form of the jonomai is perceived as cyclical because it is primarily articulated through the nohkan’s repeated patterns. The patterns are identified in the score below the video with the following legend: Chū – C, Kan – K, Kan no Chū – KC, Ryo – R, Oroshi – Or and Dan – D.

While a dance-to-text shōdan are structured around salient mimetic kata, an instrumental dance does not. Instead, particular structural kata paired with vocal calls of the percussion play a specific formal function. For instance, kata involving the fan, sometimes accompanied by sleeve motion, are used to indicate the beginning of a section. Thus, having performed the Jo and Kakari with a closed fan, the shite announces the upcoming beginning of the First dan by Opening of the Fan, the beginning of the Second dan with a Fan Reverse Hold and a Right Sleeve Flipped , and the beginning of the Third dan with a Left Sleeve Flipped. The shite signals the dance's closing phase with Renesting of the Fan and Resetting of the Sleeves. While these kata anticipate the coming dan, the actual beginnings of the dan are marked by the extended kakegoe 'iya', which within the congruent sections are reserved exclusively for this purpose.

While arm motion involving the fan and sleeves and specific kakegoe are used to announce the beginning of a section, foot motion paired with a percussion pattern are used to articulate the closing. This is done by stamping followed by the ōtsuzumi’s six consecutive strokes shikake pattern. For example, the shite concluded the kakari by performing a Left Stamp and Right Stamp followed by shikake. The closing of the First and Second dan introduce variations on the stamping and a shikake sequence.

The differences in the use of stage-space also help articulate the dance’s five-section structure. The Jo takes place exclusively on Square 1. The Kakari and the Third dan see the shite dancing accross the whole stage, whereas kata in the First and Second dan involve just a few squares.

The use of the stage-space in the kakari is worth additional note. The nohkan player introduces its core sequence of patterns called Ji that he repeats four times, while the shite opens the stage-space. Holding a closed fan in his right hand, the shite first traverses the right lateral over the first Ji – Squares 1 to 3, then the front stage over the second one – Squares 3 to 5, and finally the left-right transversal over the last two Ji – Squares 5 to 1. This trajectory does not only open the stage-space, it also creates a spatial wave of tension-release. Backstage positions such as Square 1 and 8, being further from the audience, are essentially positions of released tension. They are often used as starting and closing positions for a dance and its various sections. On the other hand, frontstage positions such as Square 3 and 4 and center-stage Square 9, being closer to the audience, are positions of strength. Therefore, the motion from Square 1 to 3 is building momentum since it brings the shite from a position of released tension in Square 1 to the front in Square 3. The motion from Square 3 to 5 maintains momentum since the dancer remains in the stage’s forefront. Finally, the motion from Square 5 to 1, releases tension, taking the shite from frontstage to backstage.

The choreography of the kakari’s exemplifies the overall modularity of Noh. The same stage-space use can be seen in both instrumental dances featured on this website: this slow and elegant jonomai, and the dynamic and virile maibataraki in Kokaji.

Another intermedia formal pointer in the jonomai is the Oroshi pattern. It is quite recognizable aurally as it is a two-honji-long slow-down, but also because the shite moves to a quieter stage position. For instance, in the First dan’s Oroshi, he takes Four Step Back to move from the dynamic Square 3 to the weaker Square 2. In the Second dan’s Oroshi , the shite performs a release-tension move by taking Seven Steps Back from the strong Square 9 to the quieter Square 6.

A large scale intermedia closure can be observed at the end of the shōdan. The last and Third dan resembles a long cadential section that takes the shite from front stage (Squares 3 and 5) to the quieter Square 2, followed with a return to front stage (Squares 3 and 5) for a last tension-release wave that takes him back to Square 9.

The shite concludes the dance by performing a Raised Fan kata. Although it can be performed anytime, it is usually seen as the shite raises attention when he sings the ageha solo line in the kuse. By performing the Raised Fan kata at the conclusion of the dance, the shite who has been silent throughout the jonomai, is by association setting up an expectation for chant. The transition is further underlined by a sudden change of tempo. Controlled by the percussionists, the tempo first gradually and steadily accelerates throughout the Third dan to reach the dance’s fastest speed at its penultimate honji. Then, it decidedly slows down in preparation for the slow tome, which in Hashitomi’s case, overlaps with the beginning of the next shōdan, the waka.

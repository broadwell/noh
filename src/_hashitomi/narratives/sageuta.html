Plot
This is a short transitional shōdan.
Text
Set in hiranori, the poetic text, based on a poem by Po Chū-i (772-846), is sung by the shite.
Music
For only the second time in the play, a vocal part is synchronized with the percussion since the congruent chant sung by the shite is supported by strict, quiet mitsuji patterns. It closes with an uchikiri.
Visuals

The shite is still singing from inside the tsukurimono.


The point of intermedia interest with the shōdan centers on how the sageuta transitions rhythmic organization, register, and mood from the issei to the following shōdan, the ageuta.

The rhythmic setting of the chant moves from the non-congruent issei to congruent. Moreover, the starting low register of the sageuta chant completes the issei chant’s descending register trajectory from high to medium, while its own motion up towards the medium range serves as a nice transition towards the high range of the next shōdan, the ageuta. The sageuta introduces also a change of density in the vocal part. From the issei’s duo for shite and jiutai, the sageuta is sung by the shite, while the following ageuta, the last shōdan of the Shite re-enters part, will be delivered by the jiutai.

Finally, as the text of the second act is gradually moving from factual information to poetry, a tone of sadness and nostalgy is gradually sinking in. The process started with the issei’s last line: “Rain dampens Genken's (Yüan Hsien) gate.”, where rain is a metaphor for tears. It is carried over in the sageuta which start with another metaphor for tears: dampened sleeves. This will then be carried over in the next shōdan, the ageuta with a reference to the limpid moon soaring over a desolate evening. In fact, one has to wait until the rongi to understand the source behind this sadness when the shite asks “When will they meet again?” , a clear yet poignant reference to her beloved Genji, whom she is still yearning for.

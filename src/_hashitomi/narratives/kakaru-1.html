Plot
In preparation for the performance of a memorial service for flowers paying a particular homage to the lotus, the priest goes to Square 9.
Text
Descriptive prose, first delivered in kotoba, then as poetic chant set in non-congruent sashinori.
Music
First entrance of the percussion instruments, providing support in flexible mode.
Visuals

The waki moves to prominent position at the center-stage and kneels down.


As the priest is about to perform a memorial service for flowers, he asserts its importance by moving to the center stage (Square 9). Coming from the energetically weaker Square 1, he opens up the stage space and activates one of the most powerful positions. Interestingly, even the shite will not enter that area in the first act limiting his actions to the quieter Squares 1 and 2. This approach is not standard. A more typical position used by other waki schools would be Square 5.

In the first three lines, the priest professes respect for the flowers, delivering his praises in kotoba. In the fourth one, the emphasis changes from flowers in general to specifically the lotus. In Buddhism, its ability to rise from murky waters to produce a beautiful and pure flower is symbolically associated with enlightenment.
The recitation of a sutra is always delivered in a sung fashion, thus the monk switches from spoken to singing mode. Reflecting on the lotus plant, he recites the Lotus Sutra: sōmoku kokudo shi’kai jōbutsudō (plants, trees, all on earth, achieve Buddhahood). The sashinori setting of the chant with its recitative style is well suited for the delivery of a religious text.

At the conclusion of the scene, the monk performs a prayer gesture similar to the one used to close the nanori. This time it has a spiritual connotation as it is performed while the priest is singing the sutra. It is excecuted with joined opened hands and rounded arms unlike in nanori when it was performed with closed fists and straight arms.The separation of hands at the end is always asymmetric.

It is interesting to note that actors stay in character even when performing an out-of-character gesture. For instance, the motion of the sleeve is technically needed to avoid wrinkling, yet it is always done in character.

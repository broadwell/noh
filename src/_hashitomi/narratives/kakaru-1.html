Plot
The monk prepares to perform a memorial service for flowers.
Text
Prose monolog of the monk about the reverence for flowers with particular homage to the lotus and announcing the intent to perform a memorial service for flowers.
Music
The vocal part of the waki is first delivered in kotoba, then chanted in yowagin style, set in non-congruent sashinori. First entrance of the percussion instruments, accompanying in flexible rhythmic setting.
Visuals

The waki moves to a prominent position at the center-stage (Square 9) and kneels down.


As the monk is about to perform a memorial service for flowers, he asserts its importance by moving to the center stage (Square 9). Coming from the energetically weaker Square 1, he opens up the stage space and activates one of the most powerful positions. Interestingly, even the shite will not enter that area in the first act limiting his actions to the quieter Squares 1 and 2. This approach is not standard. A more typical position used by other waki schools would be Square 5.

In the first three lines, the monk professes respect for the flowers, delivering his praises in kotoba. In the fourth one, the emphasis changes from flowers in general to specifically the lotus. In Buddhism, its ability to rise from murky waters to produce a beautiful and pure flower is symbolically associated with enlightenment.
The recitation of a sutra is always delivered in a sung fashion, thus the monk switches from spoken to singing mode. Reflecting on the lotus plant, he recites the Lotus Sutra: sōmoku kokudo shi’kai jōbutsudō (plants, trees, all on earth, achieve Buddhahood). The sashinori setting of the chant with its recitative style is well suited for the delivery of a religious text.

At the conclusion of the scene, the monk performs a prayer gesture similar to the one used to close the nanori. This time it has a spiritual connotation as it is performed while the monk is singing the sutra. It is excecuted with joined opened hands and rounded arms unlike in nanori when it was performed with closed fists and straight arms.The separation of hands at the end is always asymmetric.

It is interesting to note that actors stay in character even when performing an out-of-character gesture. For instance, the motion of the sleeve is technically needed to avoid wrinkling, yet it is always done in character.

Plot
The rongi is a sung dialogue between the shite and the jiutai. This is an important moment in the play because it is in this shōdan that the shite reveals her identity.
Text
The poetic text is set in hiranori.
Music
The kotsuzumi and ōtsuzumi provide a strict obbligato, while the non-congruent nohkan marks the end of the shōdan with a variation on the mi roku no ge pattern.
Visuals

The shite remains seated inside the tsukurimono until she reveals her identity, at which point she elegantly walks out of it, thus setting the expectation of an expanded use of the stage-space with the up-coming dance.


This rongi has two points of intermedia interest: The first one relates to the text and how it puts into play four characters; the second one looks at how the entrance of the nohkan helps underline the text.

Although the sung dialogue involves two protagonists: the shite and the jiutai, the text suggests the presence of four participants. First the jiutai speaks for the waki when it asks the shite to reveal her identity. Facing front as if for everyone to hear, the shite recites a poem that Lady Yūgao wrote to Genji , a reference to the Genji monogatari. As she concludes her recitation, she turns to the jiutai as if hoping for an answer as she asks “When will they ever meet again?”. As the shite turns right to face the front, the jiutai speaks for her, as it recites another poem also authored by Lady Yūgao that she addressed to her first lover, Tō no Chūjō, but it is the shite who concludes the poem’s recitation . Then, the jiutai speaks for the shite as it initiates a question for the waki, “If I reveal the form…”. At this point, the shite once again turns left to face the jiutai who now stands for the waki, as she completes the question “...will you pray for my soul ?”. The jiutai speaking for the waki affirms that he will. Upon hearing this confirmation, the shite turns right to face the full audience and reveals her identity: “the ‘evening face’”. It is at this point that the jiutai becomes the fourth character: a narrator, describing the shite coming out of the tsukurimono. As she steps out and reveals her form, the waki honors his promises and prays for her, performing the same prayer gesture he did when performing the memorial service for flowers in the first kakaru.

The nohkan player plays only once in this rongi, performing a variation on the mi roku no ge pattern as the jiutai is singing its very last line, which follows the shite stepping out of the tsukurimono . Besides bringing the corresponding text to prominence: “At the sight tears flow without stopping.”, the entrance of the nohkan also works as a smooth transition leading to an uchikiri that serves as a bridge to the next shōdan, the kuse.

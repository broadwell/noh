Synopsis
Conversation between the monk and the spirit of Lady Yūgao leading to the revealing of her identity.
Text
A poetic dialogue.
Music
A sung dialogue between the shite and the jiutai in congruent rhythm set in hiranori. The accompaniment is provided by the full ensemble with the kotsuzumi and ōtsuzumi is instrict mode and nohkan non-congruent.
Visuals

The main character remains seated inside the tsukurimono until she reveals her identity, at which point she elegantly walks out of it, thus setting the expectation of an expanded use of the stage-space with the up-coming dance.


This rongi has two points of intermedia interest: The first one relates to the text and how it puts into play four characters; the second one looks at how the entrance of the nohkan helps underline the text.

Although the sung dialogue involves two protagonists: the shite and the jiutai, the text suggests the presence of four participants.

First the jiutai speaks for the monk when it asks the main character to reveal her identity. Facing front as if for everyone to hear, the shite who personifies Lady Yūgao recites a poem that she wrote to Genji, a reference to the Genji monogatari. As she concludes her recitation, she turns to the jiutai as if hoping for an answer as she asks “When will they ever meet again?”.

As she turns right to face the front, the jiutai speaks for her, as it recites another poem also authored by Lady Yūgao that she addressed to her first lover, Tō no Chūjō, thus she concludes the poem’s recitation .

Then, the jiutai continue to speak for Lady Yūgao as it asks a question for the monk, “If I reveal the form…”. At this point, she once again turns left to face the jiutai who now stands for the monk, as she completes the question “...will you pray for my soul ?”. The jiutai speaking for the monk affirms that he will.

Upon hearing this confirmation, the woman turns right to face the full audience and reveals her identity: “the ‘evening face’”. It is at this point that the jiutai becomes the fourth character: a narrator, describing Lady Yūgao coming out of the tsukurimono. As she steps out and reveals her form, the monk honors his promises and prays for her.

The nohkan player plays only once in this rongi, performing a variation on the mi roku no ge pattern as the jiutai is singing its very last line, which follows the shite stepping out of the tsukurimono . Besides bringing the corresponding text to prominence: “At the sight tears flow without stopping.”, the entrance of the nohkan also works as a smooth transition leading to an uchikiri that serves as a bridge to the next shōdan, the kuse.

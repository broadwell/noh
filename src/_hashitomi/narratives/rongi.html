Form
Second Act - Dialogue:
Rongi
Synopsis
Dialogue between the monk and the Yūgao spirit gradually unvailing her identity.
Text
Poetry in regular rhythm.
Music
An exchange between the shite and the jiutai set in hiranori, accompanied by the percussion instruments in strict and nohkan in non-congruent rhythmic settings.
Visuals
The shite remains seated inside the tsukurimono until he elegantly walks out to fully reveal his figure.

The rongi involves two vocal parts: the shite and jiutai. While the issei-chant provided a compelling example of a single line split between three vocal parts, the rongi presents an interesting example of one part – the jiutai – lending its voice to two different characters. It speaks for the monk, the spirit and as a narrator.

For instance, the jiutai speaks for the monk when it asks the main character to reveal her form. Facing front as if for everyone to hear, the spirit of the woman rather than complying, provides a clue on her identity by reciting a poem that Lady Yūgao wrote to Genji, a reference to the Genji monogatari. As she concludes, she turns to the monk and asks “When will they ever meet again?”.

As she turns right to face the front, the jiutai speaks for her, when it starts singing another poem also authored by Lady Yūgao.

Then, once again the jiutai speaks for the woman’s spirit when it asks the monk, “If I reveal the form …” . At this point, she once again turns left to face the monk, as she completes the question “...will you pray for my soul?”. The jiutai now speaking for the monk affirms that he will.

Upon hearing this confirmation, she turns right to face the audience and mentions her name: “… ‘ the evening face’ ” . The jiutai becomes a narrator and describes the spirit of Lady Yūgao coming out of the hashitomi. As she steps out and reveals her full figure, the monk honors his promise and prays for her.

The question about the main character’s identity is at the heart of many Noh plays. It is not however their purpose. It is rather a pretext for the plot, which is usually centered around the circumstances of the encounter between a spirit and a commoner. In an unfolding story, the main character usually dances only when his/her identity has been revealed. It is in that regard that the identity of the shite’s character matters. In Hashitomi’s context, the veil around main character’s identity is lifted in the rongi, just before the dance-to-text kuse and instrumental dance jonomai.

The percussion duo and the nohkan are both used at key moments associated with the revealing of the main character’s identity. The two percussion instruments accompany the Dialogue with sparse mitsuji patterns, except for two lines, both related to the shite visual identity. For these two lines, the percussionists switch to steady and rhythmic tsuzuke patterns, producing a change in the texture that highlights the text. The first case refers to the moment when the monk asks the spirit to ”reveal her form”, while the second case comes when the jiutai speaking for the spirit, asks the monk “If I reveal the form…”, “… will you pray for my soul ?”.

The nohkan plays only once in the shōdan, performing the mi roku no ge pattern as the jiutai describes the spirit of Lady Yūgao stepping out of the hashitomi and finally revealing her form. Besides bringing the corresponding text to prominence and giving it emotional depth: “At the sight tears flow without stopping.”, the the nohkans part also overlaps with the uchikiri serving together as a smooth transition to the next shōdan, the kuse.

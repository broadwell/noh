Form
Second Act - Dialogue:
Rongi
Synopsis
Conversation between the monk and the spirit with quotations of poetry by Lady Yūgao leading to the revealing of her identity.
Text
Poetry in regular rhythm.
Music
An exchange between the shite and the jiutai set in hiranori. The kotsuzumi and ōtsuzumi’s parts in strict rhythmic setting are joined by a non-congruent nohkan part.
Visuals
The main character remains seated inside the tsukurimono until she reveals her identity, at which point she elegantly walks out, thus setting the expectation of an expanded use of the stage-space in the up-coming dance.

The intermedia intensity in the Dialogue section rises. After the spirit reveals her name, the gate of the dwelling is raised and we get to see her face (mask) and full form. The shite walks out revealing her shape and opening the stage space. He and the jiutai are now accompanied by the full hayashi with the nohkan underlining the emergence of Lady Yūgao assiting the transition to next shōdan.

The two performers also speak from four points of view. First the jiutai speaks for the monk when it asks the main character to reveal her identity. Facing front as if for everyone to hear, the shite who at this point personifies Lady Yūgao recites a poem that she wrote to Genji, a reference to the Genji monogatari. As she concludes her recitation, she turns to the jiutai as if hoping for an answer when she asks “When will they ever meet again?”. As she turns right to face the front, the jiutai speaks for her, when it recites another poem also authored by Lady Yūgao that she addressed to her first lover, Tō no Chūjō, thus she concludes the poem’s recitation .

Then, the jiutai continue to speak for Lady Yūgao as it asks a question for the monk, “If I reveal the form…”. At this point, she once again turns left to face the jiutai who now stands for the monk, as she completes the question “...will you pray for my soul ?”. The jiutai speaking for the monk affirms that he will.

Upon hearing this confirmation, the woman turns right to face the full audience and reveals her identity: “the ‘evening face’”. It is at this point that the jiutai becomes the fourth character: a narrator, describing Lady Yūgao coming out of the tsukurimono. As she steps out and reveals her form, the monk honors his promises and prays for her.

The nohkan performer plays only once in the shōdan, performing a variation on the mi roku no ge pattern as the jiutai is singing its very last line, which follows the shite stepping out of the tsukurimono . Besides bringing the corresponding text to prominence and giving it emotional depth: “At the sight tears flow without stopping.”, the entrance of the nohkan also works as a smooth transition leading to an uchikiri that serves as a bridge to the next shōdan, the kuse.

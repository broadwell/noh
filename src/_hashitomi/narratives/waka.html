Plot
At the conclusion of the jo no mai, the shite takes where he had left off at the end of the kuse, the shōdan preceding the dance, and start reciting the poem Genji sent to Lady Yūgao as a reply to hers.
Text
A waka is a genre of Japanese poetry with a prescribed syllabic structure: 5-7-5-7-7. In the context of Noh, it is a short non-congruent song. For instance in Hashitomi, it is four honji long. Its beginning overlaps with the jo no mai’s last honji.
Music
The non-congruent chant is accompanied by the otsuzumi and ōtsuzumi in strict setting.
Visuals

Following the dance which used more or less the entire stage-space, the waka represents a rest since the shite sings the chant standing on Square 8.


The short waka has two points of intermedia interest: the text assignment used to bound two protagonists, and the bridging effect of the percussion accompaniment.

The text of this waka is the poem Genji wrote in response to the verse Lady Yūgao sent him on a scented fan. The shite delivered its first line “orite koso” at the end of the kuse, but his declamation was then interrupted by the jo no mai. During the waka the shite sings the poem’s first three lines, the remaining two lines being sung by the jiutai at the beginning of the next shōdan, the kiri. Thus, Genji is personified by the shite during the waka, and by the jiutai at the beginning of the kiri.

This non-congruent waka sits between two major shōdan: the jo no mai and the kiri, and as such, it serves as a bridge between the two. Although they are both congruent, their type of congruency differs substantially. Indeed, the jo no mai’s level of congruency is quite loose compared to the kiri’s. Its slow speed paired with the highly ornamented nohkan’s melody creates a free-flowing temporal structure that is well suited for the dance of an elegant young lady, but in clear contrast with the kiri’s steady beat.

There is an interesting transformation in the waka’s percussion material that bridges the two congruent shōdan. More specifically, the percussion material subdivides the waka’s four honji in half, with the first two honji articulated with mitsuji patterns , conversely to tsuzuke patterns for the last two.

The mitsuji pattern divides the honji in two four-beat parts, the first one articulated by the ōtsuzumi with a stroke on the honji’s third beat, whereas the second half belongs to the kotsuzumi with strokes on the fifth, seventh, and eighth beats. It is the kakegoe used by the two percussionists that show that their rhythmic setting do not match. Indeed, the extended one used by the ōtsuzumi player indicates that his material is fleexible, in contrast with the strict setting of the kotsuzumi characterized by his short kakegoe. Their overlapping tsuzuke patterns starting at the third honji pulls them both into strict setting.

This transformation of the percussion material is in counterpoint with the setting of the text. Indeed, the shite delivers the first two lines of the poem over the first two honji keeping the Raised Fan in front of his face. Then, completing the interrupted Raised Fan kata, he extends the delivery of the poem’s third line over the last two honji just as the percussion material switches to tsuzuke patterns, thus stressing the importance of that line: “Tasokare ni / in the twilight”, a reference to the fact that it is in the ‘shadow of the moonlight flower’ that Genji first set sights on Lady Yūgao.

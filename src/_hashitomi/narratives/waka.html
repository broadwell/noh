Synopsis
The shite starts from where he had left off at the end of the kuse, the shōdan preceding the dance, and re-starts reciting the poem with which Genji replied to Lady Yūgao.
Text
A first half of a waka poem which will be continued in the kiri.
Music
The non-congruent chant performed by the shite in einori is accompanied by the kotsuzumi and ōtsuzumi in a strict setting. Its beginning overlaps with the jonomai’s last honji.
Visuals

The shite remains almost motionless on Square 9.


The very short waka is both a contrast and a bridge between the two major shōdan: the jonomai and the kiri. It releases tension built by the intense aestheticism of pure dance and prepares for a narrative ending.

The intermedia richness temporarily drops. After the jonomai, which had the shite perform many structural kata and use most of the stage-space, waka limits the movement to one gesture while standing still on one square. A held Raised Fan kata and the position being at the center, allows the shite to retain some energy and exert command of the stage. The music brings back chanting and keeps most energy of strict percussion rhythms but the nohkan drops off after finishing the last pattern from jonomai. The melisma of shite's non-congruent einori chant extend the dance's aesthetic focus and recalls the highly ornamented slow nohkan melody.

The percussion forms another bridge between the two rhythmically congruent but divergent shodan. The jonomai's slow speed had a free-flowing temporal feeling that was well suited for the dance of an elegant young lady. It will be in clear contrast with the steady-beat ōnori chant of the jiutai in the kiri. In the waka between them, the drums start by two honji of broad and sparse mitsuji patterns then, they acellerate toward the last two honji of tsuzuke pattern. At the same time the initial extended kakegoe of the ōtsuzumi player that seemed to imply a flexible setting are answered by short kakegoe of the kotsuzumi player. By the time they play tsuzuke patterns they are both in strict rhythm setting up the beat for ōnori.

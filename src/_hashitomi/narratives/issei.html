Synopsis
After a short entrance music, the audience hears a voice coming from the inside of the dwelling.
Text
A five-line poetic text is based on two intertwined poems from two different authors. The first three lines are delivered by the shite, the last two by the jiutai.
Music
The entrance issei-music occupies the issei’s first three of twelve honji. During the first one the rhythmic setting of percussion switches from flexible to strict. The change can be perceived by the character of the kakegoe with the frist two extended calls used by the ōtsuzumi player, reminiscent of the flexible setting, being answered by the kotsuzumi player with shorter ones, redolent of the strict setting. This switch leads to the introduction of the non-congruent nohkan’s mi roku no ge pattern, which then leads to the beginning of the chanted exchange between the shite and the jiutai. The chant in yowagin style is in non-congruent setting.
Visuals

The

tsukurimono, a symbolic dwelling is brought to life by a voice emerging from the inside.

The issei-chant introduces the first sung duo involving the jiutai. It is composed of nine honji, the first four are sang by the shite, the last five by the jiutai. Based on two different poems, the text assignment creates an interesting counterpoint as the recitation of the poems is started by one character, and completed by another one, a process already introduced in the preceding kakaru.

Kakaru

The waki sang the first two lines of Tadamoto’s poem: "Hyōtan shibashiba munashi, Kusa Gannen ga chimata shigeshi".

Issei

The shite takes over the delivery of Tadamoto’s poem as he sings its third line: "Reijō fukaku tozaseri", but rather than finishing the poem, he introduces the first two lines of Saga’s poem: "Sekiyō no zansei aratani, Mado wo ugattesaru".

Then, the jiutai completes Saga’s poem: "Shiutan no izumi no koe", before singing the last two lines of Tadamoto’s poem: "Ame Genken ga, toboso wo uruō su".

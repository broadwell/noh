Form
Second Act - Shite Re-enters:
Issei → Sageuta → Ageuta
Synopsis
After a short entrance music, a voice comes from the inside of the dwelling reciting poetry invoking the melancholic atmosphere of sunset then, sadness and tears by refering to faint sound of rain on the dwelling's gate.
Text
A five-line poetic text is based on two intertwined poems from two different authors. The first three lines are delivered by the shite, the last two by the jiutai.
Music
The entrance issei-music occupies the issei’s first three of twelve honji. During the first one the rhythmic setting of percussion switches from flexible to strict. The change can be perceived by the character of the kakegoe with the frist two extended calls used by the ōtsuzumi player, reminiscent of the flexible setting, being answered by the kotsuzumi player with shorter ones, redolent of the strict setting. This switch leads to the introduction of the non-congruent nohkan’s mi roku no ge pattern, which then leads to the beginning of the chanted exchange between the shite and the jiutai. The chant in yowagin style is in non-congruent setting.
Visuals

The ai-kyōgen discreetly leaves the stage passing behind the musicians and through the door in the back stage left. Shortly after, the

tsukurimono, a symbolic dwelling is brought to life by a voice emerging from the inside. Hearing the voice, the waki turns towards it.

The 'Shite Re-enters' section increases the intermedia richness gradually. The issei-music sees once again a development from flexible to strict rhythmic setting of the drums and then gains further presence by the arrival of the nohkan. The movement of the actors remains extremely simple but the texture gets richer as the chant introduces the sung duo involving the jiutai and the shite, and poetic texts are passed around between actors.

Issei-chant is composed of nine honji, the first four are sung by the shite, the last five by the jiutai. Based on two different poems, the text assignment creates an interesting counterpoint as the recitation of the poems is begun by one character, and completed by another one, a process started in the preceding kakaru, where the waki sang the first two lines of Tadamoto’s poem: "Hyōtan shibashiba munashi, Kusa Gannen ga chimata shigeshi". The shite takes over the delivery of the poem in issei, as he sings its third line: "Reijō fukaku tozaseri", but rather than finishing the poem, he introduces the first two lines of Saga’s poem: "Sekiyō no zansei aratani, Mado wo ugattesaru". Then, the jiutai completes Saga’s poem: "Shiutan no izumi no koe", before singing the last two lines of Tadamoto’s poem: "Ame Genken ga, toboso wo uruō su".

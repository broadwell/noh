Form
Second Act - Shite Re-enters:
Issei → Sageuta → Ageuta-2
Synopsis
The recitation of the monk that started earlier is carried on now by the voice from the inside of the dwelling.
Text
Arhythmic poetry.
Music
The entrance issei-music occupies the issei’s first three of twelve honji. During the first one the rhythmic setting of percussion switches from flexible to strict. The change can be perceived by the character of the kakegoe with the first two extended calls used by the ōtsuzumi player, reminiscent of the flexible setting, answered by the kotsuzumi player with shorter ones typical for the strict setting. This switch leads to the introduction of the non-congruent nohkan’s mi roku no ge pattern, which then leads to the beginning of the chanted exchange between the shite and the jiutai. The einori chant is delivered in yowagin style.
Visuals
After completing the previous module, the waki turns and goes back to his position on Square 5 and kneels. Meanwhile, the kyōgen discreetly leaves the stage passing behind the musicians and through the door in the back stage left. Shortly after, as the voice emerges from the inside of the tsukurimono the waki turns towards it.

The issei-music differs from the preceding shōdan with the switch in the percussion instruments’ rhythmic setting from flexible to strict, and the completing of the hayashi by the addition of the nohkan. This change prepares a further qualitative shift toward the poetic.

The issei-chant, introduces an interesting counterpoint of two poems that are intertwined and recited by two actors passing lines between them. The process starts in the preceding kakaru, where the waki sings the first two lines of the first poem. Then, the shite opens the issei-chant by singing the poem’s third line. Rather than finishing it, he introduces the first two lines of a second poem. It is the jiutai who completes the second poem with its last line, before singing the last two lines of the first. The process is summarized in this figure:

Poem 1 (Lines) Poem 2 (Lines)
Waki 1-2
Shite 3
1-2
Jiutai 3
4-5

The sharing of a poetic line (albeit composed from two poems) between three different 'actors' signals an important moment in the overall structure. It marks the definite turning away from fact-based story telling to more abstract aesthetics of poetry, music and eventually pure dance.

It is interesting to note that although the waki’s participation in the singing is over at this point in the play, he is still an active player. For instance, the moment the shite starts chanting from inside the tsukurimono the waki slides on his knee to face it. By showing that he can hear the voice he adds to its materiality despite it produced from an invisible source. Alternatively, it could indicate that everything continues to happen in the mind of the monk.

Plot
The shite who sings from inside the draped tsukurimono makes a partial entrance, as we can hear but not see her yet.
Text
The entire issei is spread over twelve honji, the non-congruent issei-chant occupies the last nine. Its five-line poetic text is based on two intertwined poems from two different authors. The first three lines are delivered by the shite, the last two by the jiutai.
Music
The entrance issei-music occupies the issei’s first three honji, and it is over the first one that the setting switches from flexible to strict. The change can be perceived by kakegoe since the extended calls used by the ōtsuzumi player, reminiscent of the flexible setting, are answered by the kotsuzumi player with shorter ones, redolent of the strict setting. This switch leads to the introduction of the non-congruent nohkan’s mi roku no gepattern, which itself leads to the beginning of the chant, a duo for shite and jiutai.
Visuals

Mysteriously, it is from inside of the tsukurimono that the issei-chant emerges.


The issei-chant introduces the first sung duo involving the jiutai. It is composed of nine honji, the first four are sang by the shite, the last five by the jiutai. The text plays a unifying function since the first line of the shite’s text carries on the rendition of Tadamoto’s poem started by the waki in the preceding kakaru.

Indeed, the shite recites the poem’s third line “Reijō fukaku tozaseri” before moving on to the first two lines of a poem by the Emperor Saga (786-842). The third and last lineof this last poem is sung by the jiutai, whose next two lines returns to and complete the initial Tadamoto’s poem. Hence, once again text is used to unite the characters.

Plot
The priest keeps on pressing Lady Yūgao to reveal her identity.
Text
The non-congruent text with a syllabic structure of 7 + 5, includes a reference to Lady Yūgao's mansion from Genji Monogatari : Chapter 4: The Twilight Beauty. This is the second clue about her identity.
Music
The dialogue between the priest and Lady Yūgao alternates between sung and spoken deliveries, over a flexible obbligato provided by the two percussion instruments.
Visuals

The use of stage-space remains static throughout the shōdan with the shite standing at Square 1, and the waki kneeling at Square 5.


The shōdan is shaped by the combination of story-telling and its percussion support.

The first act’s ‘Dialogue’ part closes with a kakaru, whose beginning is clearly marked by the return of the percussion instruments in flexible setting. The priest singing in a non-congruent fashion, presses the shite to reveal her identity. Replying in kotoba, the shite goes as far as to confirm that she is no longer alive , but keeps the mystery on her actual identity.

The conclusion of the kakaru deserves a special mention. The last sentence is “At a certain mansion, I could always be found, indeed it is so.”. This is a reference to Lady Yūgao’s mansion whose fence was covered with ‘evening face’ flowers, and as such, a clue on her identity. Thus, this line should have been delivered by the shite, yet it is the waki who sings its first part, completed by the shite in singing mode, as he turns towards the priest. It is interesting that as the mystery on Lady Yūgao’s identity was about to be partially lifted, the text assignment ends up creating some ambiguity.

This important clue on the shite’s identity coincides with a switch in the percussion material from sparse mitsuji to the regular tsuzuke pattern , a move that adds momentum to the music and call the listener to attention, as important information is being presented.

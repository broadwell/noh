Plot
The monk persists on asking the apparition to reveal her identity.
Text
The descriptive dialogue between the monk and the apparition in a traditional syllabic structure of 7 + 5. Includes a reference to Lady Yūgao's mansion from Genji Monogatari : Chapter 4: "The Twilight Beauty." This is the second clue about her identity.
Music
The dialogue between the waki and shite alternates between spoken and chanted, accompanied by percussion instruments in flexible rhythmic setting.
Visuals

The use of stage-space remains static with the shite standing at Square 1, and the waki kneeling at Square 5.


The shōdan is shaped by the combination of story-telling and its percussion support.

The first act’s ‘Dialogue’ part closes with a kakaru, whose beginning is clearly marked by the return of the percussion instruments in flexible setting. The monk singing in a non-congruent fashion, presses the shite to reveal her identity. Replying in kotoba, the shite goes as far as to confirm that she is no longer alive , but keeps the mystery on her actual identity.

The conclusion of the kakaru deserves a special mention. The last sentence is “At a certain mansion, I could always be found, indeed it is so.”. This is a reference to Lady Yūgao’s mansion whose fence was covered with ‘evening face’ flowers, and as such, a clue on her identity. Thus, this line should have been delivered by the woman, yet it is the monk who sings its first part, completed by the woman in singing mode, as she turns towards the monk. It is interesting that as the mystery on Lady Yūgao’s identity was about to be partially lifted, the text assignment ends up creating some ambiguity.

This important clue on the shite’s identity coincides with a switch in the percussion material , moving from sparse mitsuji to the regular tsuzuke pattern, a move that adds momentum to the music and call the listener to attention, as important information is being presented.

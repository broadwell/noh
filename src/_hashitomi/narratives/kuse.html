Plot
Based on the text’s structure, the kuse can be divided up into three parts. Part I introduces Lady Yūgao and her short affair with Genji. Part II reveals how a plucked flower delivered on a white fan brought them together, while Part III provides the details on how the white ‘evening face’ flower served as a bridge that united the two lovers.
Text
The poetic text set in hiranori includes two references to Genji Monogatari, both from letters written by Lady Yūgao. It is sung by the jiutai with one transitional line (ageha) sung by the shite.
Music
The kotsuzumi & ōtsuzumi are in strict setting, while the non-congruent nohkan part includes two utterances.
Visuals

The kuse is the first dance-to-text in the play. The expanded use of the stage-space follows the overall three-part subdivision of the shōdan. In Part-I the performance of most kata happens on Square 9. The use of stage-space is extended to Squares 2 and 3 in Part-II, and finally to the stage’s right lateral (Square 1, 2, and 3) and center (Squares 4, 8, and 9) during Part-III.

This kuse has three points of intermedia interest: The first one focuses on how mimicking kata are used to provide the text with a visual representation. The second looks at how the text changes the shite’s personification. Finally, the third point considers how various techniques are used to emphasize the text, including the use of range, rhythmic patterns, and measures. For instance, the introduction of a four-beat measure (toriji) before an eight-beat measure (honji) helps emphasize and highlight important words from the narrative, since it provides the text with twenty-four syllabic positions, a number that usually far exceed the text’s needs. The outcome is that rather than spreading the text over the increased number of beats, a larger than usual number of unsung beats is added before the line, where the resuming declamation ends up highlighting its first few words.


Intermedia Part-I

The flower ritual that the monk is performing brings back memories to Lady Yūgao about the prayer to Buddha that she and Genji heard the very first night they spent together. Part I is divided up into two sub-parts: The first one provides context as the jiutai is narrating the story, whereas in the second sub-part refers to Lady Yūgao’s emotions.

The text of the first sub-part introduces Genji and his short romance with Lady Yūgao. As it is common for a kuse, the melody starts in the low range when a Left Stamp on the seventh beat of the third honji triggers a short and sudden incursion in the high range over the words “Tada karibushi no yo mo sugara/Through the night they heard from next door a pilgrim’s voice in prayer”, thereby emphasizing the importance of the heard prayer. Then a Right Stamp on the seventh beat of the following honji triggers a return to the medium and subsequently low range.

At this point, the text refers to the “pilgrim’s voice in prayer”, hence the shite makes a Right Step pivot and takes Three Steps Forward and one Step Backward as to better position herself to listen to the prayer. She then performs a ‘Mind listening’ kata by bending her upper body forward.

As the jiutai is about to recite the sutra “Namutōraidōshi Mirokub’to zo tonaekeru”, the kotsuzumi player plays his hajiki pattern which include an off-beat attack that immediately follows the ōtsuzumi’s stroke on the first beat. This rarely used pattern with its oddly positioned attack creates a bouncing effect that calls for the listener's attention, preparing the mood for the coming prayer. The sutra includes 21 syllables, set in a sequence of four measures: toriji (4-beat measure), kataji (6-beat measure), toriji (4-beat measure), and a honji (8-beat measure), for a total of 22 beats or 44 syllable-slots. As an outcome of this larger than needed number of beats, the delivery speed of the text slows down , an appropriate motion for the recitation of a prayer.


Intermedia Part-II

As it is standard in dances, the Opening Fan kata marks the beginning of the dance’s next section, in this case Part-II. The expanding use of the space is also indicative of the beginning of a new section. Indeed, while the performance of kata in Part-I was located around Square 9, it expands to Squares 2 and 3 in Part II.

In the first sentence of Part II, Lady Yūgao tells the priest about her first encounter with Genji, hence it positioned her in the present. Lady Yūgao reminisces about three specific moments, each one shares the same melodic shape and the same transformation in its mode of delivery. More specifically, each of the three melodic lines start in the medium range and cadence in the low, and each one starts in the declamatory mitsuji-utai accompanied by sparse mitsuji patterns before switching to tsuzuke-utai accompanied by steady tsuzuke patterns.

Lady Yūgao’s first memories refers to the moment Genji first saw her house. The second one refers to Genji beckoning his foster brother Koremitsu to pick up the ‘evening face’ flower for him. By performing the Beckoning Fan kata at the same instant as she recalls the moment, she becomes Genji. Then, the jiutai speaking Genji’s words orders his foster brother to pick up the white flower. By extending the fan and turning to face the tsukurimono, which represents Lady Yūgao’s dwelling, Genji clearly identifies the flower his foster brother is to pluck.

Finally, the last and third memories refers to the white fan that foreshadows Genji and Lady Yūgao’s encounter. According to the Genji Monogatari, a young servant girl stepped out of the house and provided a guard rather than Koremitsu with a scented white fan and told him, as he was about to pluck the flowers, “Here give them to him on this – their stems are so hopeless”. Although unspoken, this moment of the story is summarized with the shite turning to face front and performing the Offering the Fan kata, hence for that short moment, the shite is that young servant. Then the jiutai tells that the flower was picked and presented. Hence, the shite becomes Koremitsu as he performs a sequence of mimicking kata: Left Hand pick up of the flower, Laying of the flower on the fan, Kneeling and the Two Hands Offering of the Fan.


Intermedia Part-III

The jiutai speaking for Lady Yūgao, tells that it is while contemplating a flower at her gate that Genji noticed her in the distance, and that their love bloomed out of a flower presented on a fan. From that moment, the jiutai starts speaking for Genji and alludes to the poem he wrote on the fan, but the narration is interrupted by the elegant dance: jo no mai. Hence, one has to wait until the next shōdan , the waka to hear Genji’s lovely poem.

The beginning of Part III is clearly marked by the shite’s ageha, his sole vocal line in the shōdan. As he sings in Kneeling Position and gazing at his own fan “Genji, gazing at it intently”, we understand that the shite is now personifying Genji.

The jiutai’s reply coincides with the entrance of the nohkan’s pattern takane mi kusari. It starts with a five-syllable line stretched over the honji’s eight beats, thereby emphasizing its text “Of you passing in the distance…”. The shite who performs at the same time a Large Zigzag, as if passing in the distance, becomes Lady Yūgao. The kata takes her from Square 2 to the dynamic Square 4 where she concludes with a Scooping Point followed by an Open-retreat, thereby opening Part III by further expanding the use of the stage-space.
The melodies corresponding to the text “Of you passing in the distance…” and “… and if I had not answered about the flower” are both set in the declamatory mitsuji-utai, which has the effect of increasing momentum and adding a dramatic stress to these words. The consequence to that “and if...”, is then spelt out in a line of twelve syllables delivered in tsuzuke-utai set over a honji preceded by a toriji (four-beat measure), hence creating a slowdown in the declamation that emphasizes the dramatic consequence: “forever we must have remained strangers.”.

The dance is also gaining in momentum with the kata Take the Corner that brings the main character to another strong position: Square 3 where over the words: "by the fan touched by our hands" she mimics the text with a Fan to the Left hand kata. After a Large Left Circle (Squares 3 – 9 -1), this sequence of movements comes to a rest on Square 1.

An interesting identity switch takes place at this point as the jiutai starts singing Genji's words. Thus, once on Square 1, and over the lines “…in a joyous pledge of love…”, the shite performs a Feather Fan kata, as he takes Nine Steps towards the waki. By offering him the fan, he becomes Genji, while the waki for this brief moment stands for Lady Yūgao.

Finally, the shite initiates the closing sequence that will take him center stage after having traversed the front stage lateral. The sequence starts with a Renesting the Fan, followed with a dynamic Take the Corner with Extended Fan, and a Large Circle (3-5-9) that takes him to Square 9, in front of the tsukurimono, where he performs a Small Zigzag followed by a Closure scoop , a standard sequence of kata used for closing a section.

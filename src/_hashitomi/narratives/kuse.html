Form
Second Act - Shite Performs:
Kuse → Jonomai
Synopsis
Poetic account of scenes from the romantic encounter of Lady Yūgao and Prince Genji. It includes the lovers overhearing Buddhist chanting at night as well as being introduced by a yūgao flower delivered on a white fan.
Text
Poetry in irregular rhythm.
Music
The chant set in hiranori is sung in yowagin style by the jiutai with one line (ageha) by the shite. It is accompanied by the percussion instruments in strict and nohkan in non-congruent rhythmic settings.
Visuals
The kuse is the first module in the play that extensively uses dance-to-text movement. The gradual expanded use of the stage-space follows the overall three-part subdivision of the shōdan. In Part-I the performance of most kata happens on Square 9. The use of stage-space is extended to Squares 2 and 3 in Part-II, and finally during Part-III, it includes also center Squares 4, 8, and 9.

There are usually two dance-to-text shōdan in a play: the kuse and the kiri. Intermedia relationships are typically at their height in these shōdan as their text is correlated with mimetic movements, with dramatic use of stage-space and with appropriate accompanying music.

When the spirit of Lady Yūgao (sung by jiutai) recounts that during her first night with Genji the lovers heard from next door a pilgrim’s voice in prayer, the shite performs a slow sequence of steps that leads to the subtle Mind Listening kata, done by slighly bending the upper body forward. As the jiutai is about to recite the sutra the kotsuzumi plays a hajiki pattern which includes an off-beat attack that immediately follows the ōtsuzumi’s stroke on the first beat. This rarely-used pattern with its oddly-positioned attack creates a syncopated effect that creates a sort of "quotation mark" before the prayer begins. The sutra includes 21 syllables, set in a sequence of four uneven measures (4, 6, 4 and 8 beats long). This allows for a spacious distribution of syllables and slow pacing appropriate for a prayer.

Moved by the monk's service for the flowers, Lady Yūgao shows him a profound appreciation by taking Five Steps in his direction, then One Step Back, before performing the Kneeling in Prayer. As the jiutai sings: “… unwittingly tears dampen my sleeves” the shite performs Left-Hand Weeping kata. This poignant moment ends with the two percussion instruments playing the standard closing uchikiri pattern.

Mimesis can take a different form when abstract structural kata are given meaning by the text. For instance, when the jiutai sings “When Genji first saw this house”, the shite performs a standard Forward pointing kata but because it is directed towards the tsukurimono it implies Prince Genji's pointing to Lady Yūgao's house.

A moment later, we hear that Genji beckons his personal attendant Koremitsu to pick a flower for him that grows over the house. The shite, now personifies Koremitsu who carries out the order. It appears as a mimetic kata sequence: Left hand pick up of the flower, Laying of the flower on the fan, Kneeling and the Two hands offering of the fan. The moment of offering the flower on the fan is given dramatic emphasis by a significant slow-down of the music, addition of an extra measure of just 4 beats and the ageha, the short and sole vocal entrance of the shite during the whole kuse

The text of the ageha refers to the flower on the fan, as the shite sings “Genji, gazing at it intently”. The shite, by looking intently at his fan personifies Genji. Finally, as the jiutai sings words attributable to Genji “Of you passing in the distance...” the shite performs a Large Zigzag interpretable as the walk of Lady Yūgao. The intensity of the moment when Genji first saw Lady Yūgao, is underlined by the entrance of the nohkan’s takane mi kusari pattern.

The text divides the narrative into three parts, each one marked by gradual increase in the use of stage-space. The first part introduces Genji and his romance with Lady Yūgao, as she recalls the prayer that they heard on their first night. The introductory quality of the narrative is reflected in limiting the stage-space to the center-stage (Square 9). The tone of the second part intensifies slightly as the spirit of Lady Yūgao remembers that her introduction to Genji was initiated by his interest in a flower. The stage-space reflects the growing intensity by adding movement on Squares 2 and 3. We learn in the third part, that it is while contemplating a flower at Lady Yūgao’s gate that Genji noticed her in the distance, and that their romantic encounter started with a flower presented on a fan. The pivotal nature of the moment is paralleled by the use of almost the entire stage-space.

Synopsis
A local villager enters into a conversation with the monk and tells the story of the romantic encounter of Lady Yūgao with Prince Genji.
Text
Factual dialogue in common Japanese.
Music
A kyōgen style of recitation of the ai-kyōgen alternating with kotoba phrases of the waki. Close to the end of the dialogue, the nohkan player joins in with the shimo no takane pattern. It is used exclusively to accompany the dialogue between waki and ai-kyōgen. It also serves as a signal to the actors in backstage that the second act is about to start.
Visuals
After entering the stage and a short exchange with the waki from Square 1, the kyōgen actor moves to center stage at Square 8, where he kneels to deliver his monologue. At the early and late parts of the module when ai and waki converse they turn toward each other.

The ai-kyōgen interlude in Hashitomi has a typically simple intermedia character. The kyōgen actor converses with the waki with very little movement and mostly without music.

In preparation for the ai-kyōgen, the percussionists fold their chairs, as all of the musicians kneel in a straight line facing sideways to indicate that although they are on stage they are not involved.

However, half way through the interlude, when the ai-kyōgen turns back to the waki to re-enter into a dialogue, the drummers return to sitting on the chairs. It is the tradition of the Morita school of nohkan playing to layer the final section of the ai-kyōgen with a melodic pattern called shimo no takane. It is a non-congruent melody that includes few patterns that are specific to the shimo no takane, but also others borrowed from mi roku no ge, takane hane, and kuse-no-ryo. The end of the nohkan’s melody must coincide more of less with the end of the dialogue between the kyōgen and the waki. Having memorized the text, the nohkan player positions his patterns accordingly.

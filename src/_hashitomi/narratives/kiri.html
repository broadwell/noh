Plot
The very last module (shōdan) of a play, the kiri is a sung dialogue between the shite and the chorus, and a dance-to-text. It can be divided into two parts: Part-I acts as a bridge between the waka (the preceding module) and the kiri proper. Part-II includes the call and response between the two singing bodies and the dance-to-text.
Text
Because of its periodic quality, the ōnori text setting is often used after a dance.
Music
The kotsuzumi & ōtsuzumi are in strict setting, whereas the nohkan's part is non-congruent.
Visuals

Except for the very end, the dance occupies the three strongest stage positions: Squares 9, 4, and 3.


The points of intermedia interest in the kiri center on rhythmic setting to emphasize important text, and on how mimicking kata are used to provide the text with a visual representation, a technique also used in the kuse.

Hashitomi’s kiri is preceded by a waka during which the shite, standing for Genji, recited the first three lines of the poem he wrote to Lady Yūgao, the night he first laid eyes on her. The kiri’s Part-I opens up with the jiutai, now standing for Genji, delivering the last two lines of the same poem, while the shite standing center stage performs a sequence of opening kata: Medium zigzag, Forward pointing, and an Open-retreat. The jiutai repeats three times the poem’s last line: “hana no iugao /the flowering “evening face””, thus stressing the words “iugao / evening face’, the nickname Genji gave to the lady he saw in the shadow of the white flower. The text is not only emphasized through repetition but also through its rhythmic setting. Indeed, its seven syllables laid over a honji for the first two iterations , are spread over two for the third utterance.

Part-II opens up with the first out of three interventions by the shite. The first two exchanges between the shite and the jiutai carry the farewell words of Lady Yūgao. The shite standing center stage and speaking for Lady Yūgao delivers a two-honji long solo on a single pitch with a slow steady pulse, accompanied by sparse mitsuji patterns by the two hand-percussion instruments. As the jiutai, also speaking for Lady Yūgao, replies with a one-honji long melodic line of three pitches sung at a much faster speed and accompanied by steady percussion’s tsuzuke patterns, the shite performs a Simple Left Pivot to face the waki as the jiutai is begging him to pray for her. Then, as the jiutai is announcing the coming of dawn, the shite takes three steps towards the strong Square 4 in preparation for the last exchange which leads into the dance-to-text.

The last exchange between the shite and the jiutai puts in parallel the coming of dawn with the disappearance of Lady Yūgao, just like Genji who after having spent the night with her, would disappear in the early morning hours. Like her two preceding vocal lines, this one is also rhythmically and melodically static. Once again, the percussion instruments use sparse mitsuji patterns to support the shite’s solo. Yet, the kotsuzumi’s patterns include upbeat attacks that either lead to first or fifth beat of the honji, and although these patterns are not idiosyncratic to Hashitomi's kiri, their loose synchronization with the words "Kane mo shikiri ni / the tolling of the bells" suggests that they could be read as mimicking the rhythmic motion of the bells .

In Noh, east is associated with the stage's left side, so it is over the words: "Kane mo shikiri ni. /The tolling of the bells”, that the shite turns left and looks up to appreciate the rising sun. Then with a Forward Point, she points in the direction of the bells and performs a Flipped Left Sleeve and a Cloud Fan as he sings “Tsuge wataru shinonome. / …announce the coming of dawn.”, as this pattern is often used to suggest looking into the distance.

From this point on, the jiutai, who is now acting as a narrator, announces the coming of dawn and the end of the play. After Resetting his Left Sleeve , the shite initiates his closing segment by opening up the dance space, with a Right Circle that takes him back to center stage Square 9. Then, after having Taken seven steps to Square 3, where he performs a Righgt SStep Pivot, thus facing the tsukurimono, he performs a Forward Point with an Open-retreat, as if identifying his final destination.

After having Taken eight steps to Square 8, he enters the tsukurimono as the nohkan that had not been heard yet, announces the imminent end of the play with its closing Tome pattern. At about the same time, the shite moves his Fan to Left Hand in preparation for a Pillow Fan, a pattern that suggests disappearance, that he performs as the jiutai sings “sono mama yume to zo nari ni keru. / …becoming one with the dream.”.

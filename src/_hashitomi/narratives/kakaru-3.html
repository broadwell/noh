Plot
The waki still kneeling at Square 5 stands up, turns towards the tsukurimono , and makes two steps in its direction to indicate that he has made the trip to the gojō atari, Lady Yūgao’s living area referred to in the ageuta , the last shōdan of the first act. The priest is now facing what used to be the entrance to the Lady Yūgao’s house .
Text
The syllabic structure of the poetic text 7 + 5 (for the most part), sung in a non-congruent fashion, is set in sashinori.
Music
The waki’s chant is supported by a flexible obbligato performed the two hand-percussion instruments.
Visuals

The waki remains standing at Square 5.


The point of intermedia interest in this kakaru centers on the relationship between the text and its rhythmic accompaniment.

At the sight of the neglected dwelling, the waki recites a poem by Tachibana no Tadamoto (10th century), who wrote about two Confucius disciples Gannen and Genken, who pursued their spiritual studies in great poverty. The poem refers to hyōtan a variety of iugao that looks like small gourds, where the sentence ‘gourds are often empty’ is a metaphor for poverty.

The kakaru is spread over five eight-beat measures (honji). Half-way in the third one, the kotsuzumi player initiate a switch from flexible to strict setting , a motion clearly perceptible by his much shorter kakegoe. At this point, the percussionists also switch from the sparse mitsuji to the more dynamic tsuzuke patterns. This change in the mode of performance coincides more or less with the beginning of the waki’s recitation of Tadamoto’s poem, thereby prompting the listener to attention.

Form
Second Act - Waki Waits:
Kakaru-3
Synopsis
The monk arrives at Gojō to visit the one-time home of Lady Yūgao. He observes that the dwelling is still covered by the same yūgao flowers but overgrown with weeds. He begins to recite a poem that references spiritual learning and poverty.
Text
Arhythmic poetry.
Music
Sashinori chant delivered by the waki in yowagin style, accompanied by the kotsuzumi and ōtsuzumi’s parts in a flexible rhythmic setting.
Visuals
After kneeling at Square 5, the waki stands up, turns towards the tsukurimono, which represents Lady Yūgao's dwelling. He makes two steps in its direction indicating that he has made the trip to Gojō, where Lady Yūgao lived.

The first module after the interlude is the beginning of the 'Waki Waits' section and the final energetic and intermedia arc. It will last for the full second act. It starts relatively simply with the waki singing accompanied by the drums in non-congruent style. But as he begins to quote a poem the relationship between the text and its rhythmic accompaniment coalesces and energy rises. Still, the overall expression is centered on a poem about a Confucian’s humble house.

The kakaru is spread over five eight-beat measures (honji). Half-way in the third one, the kotsuzumi player initiates a switch from flexible to strict setting , a motion recognizable by his much shorter kakegoe. At this point, the percussionists also switch from the sparse mitsuji to the more dynamic tsuzuke patterns. This change in the mode of performance coincides more or less with the beginning of the waki’s recitation of Tadamoto’s poem.

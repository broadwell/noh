Plot
The monk arrives at Gojō to visit the one-time dwelling of Lady Yūgao.
Text
Poetic monolog of the monk with a syllabic structure of mostly 7 + 5. Observes that the dwelling is still covered by the same iugao flowers but empty and overgrown with weeds.
Music
The waki chants in yowaginstyle, set in non-congruent rhythmic mode in sashinori, and accompanied by the percussion instruments in a flexible rhythmic setting.
Visuals
After kneeling at Square 5 until now, the waki stands up, turns towards the tsukurimono , and makes two steps in its direction. This indicates that he has made the trip to Gojō, where Lady Yūgao lived as referred to in the last shōdan of the first act. He faces the draped tsukurimono, which represents Lady Yūgao's dwelling.

The point of intermedia interest in this kakaru centers on the relationship between the text and its rhythmic accompaniment.

At the sight of the neglected dwelling, the monk recites a poem written by Tachibana no Tadamoto (10th century) about two Confucius disciples Gannen and Genken, who pursued their spiritual studies in great poverty. The poem refers to hyōtan a variety of iugao that looks like small gourds, where the sentence ‘gourds are often empty’ is a metaphor for poverty.

The kakaru is spread over five eight-beat measures (honji). Half-way in the third one, the kotsuzumi player initiate a switch from flexible to strict setting , a motion clearly perceptible by his much shorter kakegoe. At this point, the percussionists also switch from the sparse mitsuji to the more dynamic tsuzuke patterns. This change in the mode of performance coincides more or less with the beginning of the waki’s recitation of Tadamoto’s poem, thereby prompting the listener to attention.

Plot
While the monk is performing the service for the flowers he notices the smiling white flower among his lot.
Text
The unmetered dialogue between the monk and the flower/spirit of Lady Yūgao includes references to the moment Prince Genji met Lady Yūgao in Genji Monogatari : Chapter 4: "The Twilight Beauty." As the spirit introduces herself as the ‘evening face’ flower (iugao in Japanese), the monk inquires about its provenance and receives a hint that he may be speaking to a spirit.
Music
An unaccompanied exchange between waki and shite first in speaking, then in chanting yowagin mode.
Visuals

The waki remains kneeling at Square 5 while the shite walks from the First Pine to Square 1. First he turns toward the audience, then toward the waki.


The scene is shaped by the combination of story-telling, stage movement and changing modes of text delivery .

The monk opens the shōdan delivering his line in kotoba. We surmise that he is in the process of dedicating flowers to Buddha, when he expresses surprise, noticing for the first time a smiling white flower among his lot. He turns toward the woman and asks her about it. Her reply in singing mode adds an aura of mystery and beauty to her statement. As she sings she continues to slowly come closer to the monk. In fact, after arriving at Square 1 she further delays coming in visual contact with him, by first facing the audience and only later does she turn toward the monk and identifies the white flower as the ‘evening face’ (iugao in Japanese). At this point, it is in singing mode that the monk asks the woman to identify herself . Now facing front to engage the audience as much as the monk, she replies in a spoken mode, that she is not ready to reveal her identity.The change in the mode of delivery from singing to speaking puts an emphasis on her text, and stresses the fact that she is not yet ready to state her name. Yet, turning once again to face the monk she drops a clue , stating that she comes from the white flower’s shadow.

There is in fact a beautiful double entendre in her reply. Prince Genji who was on his way to visit one of his mistresses could not help but stop when he noticed a beautiful white flower on a fence in front of a dilapidated house. As he was admiring the flower, he noticed a beautiful woman in the house. It is as the ‘shadow’ of the white flower that the protagonist of Hashitomi met Prince Genji, the same way as she has just been introduced to the monk. She became known as Lady Yūgao, named after the flower. Her real name is never mentioned in the novel.

In comparison to most plays, the shite's self-introduction happens rather early in this play. Still, the text remains ambiguious enouhg to maintain the spirit of mystery, a characteristic which is at the heart of almost all Noh plays.

Form
First Act - Shite Enters:
Ashirai → Song
Synopsis
While the monk is performing the memorial service a woman appears from a flower's shadow, as if in a dream.
Text
n/a
Music
Entrance music with nohkan in non-congruent and the kotsuzumi and ōtsuzumi's parts in flexible rhythmic settings.
Visuals
The waki moves from the powerful center-stage (Square 9) to his resting post at (Square 5), at which point the shite starts a slow entrance concluded at the Second Pine. The red in the costume suggests that the character is a young woman.

The preceding 'Waki Enters' section has set the scene for the arrival of the main actor. The chanting ceased and the waki emptied the space by moving from the center of the stage to his post on Square 5. With very deliberate walking movement of both actors accompanied only by instrumental music the intermedia texture becomes plain and sparse again. 'Shite Enters' section and the second arc of gradual dramatic and intermedia build-up is beginning. It will be longer than the first and it will reach its peak in the following "Dialogue" section.

Overlapping from the previous scene, a very slow percussion music accompanies the waki as he walks to his pole. Its expression together with waki's gait helps create anticipation for the arrival of shite. The main character will appear seemingly out of nowhere. The nohkan begins to play at the second honji. Its haunting melody ‘summons’ the apparition and helps define its enigmatic beauty. Its reserved ornamentation sets a humble yet refined tone. This is the first module involving the nohkan and featuring all musicians.

The timing and gesture of curtain lifting depends on the shite's cue. After waiting for the right moment, the way the shite utters omaku suggests to the stage assistant the manner in which the lifting is to be executed. In the featured performance the shite waited until the third honji, which gave more time for the music to set the proper mood.

As the shite enters the bridge, the curtain goes down as slowly as it was lifted. He walks very slowly, taking two minutes to reach the First Pine. This delays the moment of showing the mask in full face. Only as he is about to sing, does he turn toward the audience, thus revealing the mask that can be admired as the entrance music is coming to an end. Not entering the stage proper may imply that the apparition remains invisible to the monk.

For the entire shōdan, the free-flowing nohkan’s melody is supported by an equally free percussion accompaniment consisting only of sparse mitsuji patterns played in flexible mode. The absence of periodic pulsations provides the music with an unsettling feeling, which matches well the spirit of a dream and an otherworldly appearance.

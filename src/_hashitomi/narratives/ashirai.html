Plot
The yet unidentified spirit of Lady Yūgao appears while the priest is performing the mass for the flowers.
Text
None
Music
Non-congruent entrance music. The is the first section featuring all musicians. The music is very slow, setting a humble yet refined tone for the entrance of the shite
Visuals

The waki moves from Square 9 to his resting post at Square 5, at which point the shite starts her slow entrance that concludes at the First pine. Her red elegantly designed costume suggests that her character is a young woman from a good family. The patterns of flowers and fences on the front will gain their full meaning as the narrative reveals it.


The entrance of the shite is preceded by very slow but intense music that builds up anticipation of the mysterious appearance. Only the percussion accompanies the waki as he walks to his pole. The expression of his gait serves as a preparation, it helps establish the right energy to bring in the shite. The nohkan begins to play with the second honji. It’s haunting melodic patterns ‘summon’ the apparition and help establish its mysterious beauty. The timing and gesture of curtain lifting depends on the shite. After waiting for the right moment, the way the shite utters ‘omaku’ suggests to the stage assistant the manner, in which the lifting is to be executed. In the featured performance the shite waited for the third honji, which gave more time for the music to do its work. He walks extremely slowly and makes his appearance more gradual by not facing the audience for the first three minutes and showing only side of the mask. Upon him reaching the First Pine the curtain goes down as slowly as it was lifted. Only as the shite prepares to sing he turns toward the audience. An arrival point at the First Pine in a middle of a honji and full revealing of the mask that then is admired during an additional closing honji, is a typical variant of a shite entrance.

For the entire shōdan, the free-flowing nohkan’s melody is supported by an equally free percussion material consisting only of sparse mitsuji patterns played in flexible mode. The absence of periodic pulsations provides the music with an unsettling feeling, which matches well the spirit of a dream and an otherworldly appearance.

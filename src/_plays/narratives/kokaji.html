Synopsis

After having had a dream about a powerful sword, the 11th century emperor Ichijō sends an envoy to the swordsmith Kokaji Munechika requesting that he forges that sword. Munechika, who does not have an assistant with the proper skills to help him create that sword, goes to the Inari shrine to pray. There a boy comes to him and tells him that help is coming. Munechika goes back home where the deity from the Inari shrine appears and helps him forge the requested sword.

Act I

Waki’s enters

The waki's entrance part is spread over three modules (shōdan), the first two are non-congruent, while the third and last one is congruent.
It is the wakizure who first enters the stage without any accompaniment. Upon reaching Square 1, he delivers an unaccompanied spoken monologue (nanori's analysis).
Then, a dialogue between the wakizure and waki (mondō-1) precedes a congruent chant.
Set in hiranori, it is accompanied by the two hand-percussion in strict setting and a non-congruent nohkan’s part. This type of shōdan called an ageuta is often used to close the Waki- and Shite-enters sections of a play (ageuta-1).

Shite’s enters

In most Noh plays, the arrival on stage of the principal actor – the shite, is announced with an entrance music. In Kokaji the shite enters section is unusual as the arrival of the shite – a boy appearing out of thin air, is meant to be unexpected. Thus, to assure the surprise effect there is no entrance music, rather it starts with a two-part introduction: a short spoken exchange between the wakizure and the waki (dialogue), followed by a spoken soliloquy delivered by the waki (monologue).
This leads to the shite’s entrance which comes as an unexpected call from the distance , called yobikake .

Dialogue

The Dialogue section is spread over two shōdan. The yubikake merges with a dialogue in prose between the shite and waki (mondō-2).
It is followed by the second congruent chant of the play, another ageuta. Set in hiranori, it is delivered by the jiutai, accompanied by the two-hand percussion instruments in strict rhythmic setting and a non-congruent nohkan's part (ageuta-2).

The Shite performs

The first act’s Shite performs section is composed of three shōdan. It starts with a dynamic non-congruent chant sung by the jiutai, accompanied by the percussion in strict rhythmic setting and a non-congruent nohkan’s part (kuri).
The overall level of congruency is lowered with the next module, a recitative-like chant sung by the shite and jiutai, accompanied by the percussion in flexible rhythmic setting and a non-congruent nohkan’s part (sashi).
The dance-to-text shōdan that follows substantially raises the play's overall level of congruency. This is where the plot kernel is given and as such it is the first act’s point of focus. Sung by the shite and jiutai, it is set in hiranori, accompanied by the percussion instruments set in strict rhythm and a non-congruent nohkan’s part. It is divided up into two parts. The shite is kneeling center-stage for the first one, but dances during the last one (kuse).

Shite exits

The Shite exits section counts three shōdan. It opens with a dialogue in prose between the shite and waki whose last line is sung rather than spoken (kakeai-1).
It is followed by the first act’s third ageuta, a congruent chant set in hiranori performed by the jiutai and accompanied by the percussion in strict rhythmic setting and a non-congruent nohkan's part (ageuta-3).
The first act is then concluded with an exit music that accompanies the shite and waki’s exit, as well as the kyogen actor’s entrance. It is performed by the three percussionists and the nohkan player. The rhythmic setting is flexible during the exit of the two protagonists, but becomes strict for the entrance of the kyōgen (raijo).


Interlude

The nakairi or short performance between the acts, traditionally performed by a kyōgen actor, has a dual function: first, it allows time for the shite to change mask and costume to transform into the second-act character; second, it helps clarify the story. The kyōgen is usually unmasked and engaged in a discussion with the waki, but the situation is different in Kokaji. First the kyōgen is masked since he personifies a deity from a lower-ranked shrine. Moreover, he addresses the audience since the waki has left the stage for a change of costume.


Act II

Waki waits

The second act opens up with the waki performing a notto, a non-congruent chant, sung by the waki in sashinori style, accompanied by a steady percussion beat (notto's analysis).
It is followed by a congruent chant, set in ōnori accompanied by a percussion part set in strict rhythm. It is introduced by the waki but sang by the jiutai (noriji-1).

Shite re-enters

The Shite re-enters section includes only one shōdan, an extremely dynamic entrance music , with the two-hand percussion instruments and taiko in strict rhythmic setting and the very first congruent nohkan part in the play (hayafue).

Dialogue

There is no Dialogue section in Kokaji’s second act.

Shite performs

The second act’s Shite performs includes three shōdan. The second one is the instrumental dance , thus the highlight of the play (maibataraki).
It is flanked on each side by the second , and third congruent chants set in ōnori.
Accompanied by the percussion set in strict rhythm, the first one also includes a non-congruent nohkan part (noriji-2).
The jiutai who sings the first one is joined by the shite in the second one (noriji-3).

Shite exits

The second act’s Shite exits section includes two shōdan. It starts with a short spoken dialogue between the waki and shite, accompanied by the two hand-percussion instruments in flexible rhythmic setting (kakeai-2).
It is followed by the second and last dance-to-text module. Sung by the jiutai and shite, it is set in chūnori with a percussion part in strict rhythm and a non-congruent nohkan part (kiri).

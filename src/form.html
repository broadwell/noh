Form

Zeami is credited with having perfected Noh as it still exists today. When it came to form, he adapted the Gagaku concept of modulation and movement called Jo-ha-kyū. The tripartite can be summarized as: Jo - slow, Ha - development / acceleration, and kyū - fast conclusion, suggesting that most efforts should start slowly, speed up, before rapidly ending. Eventually, the principle of Jo-ha-kyū was applied to various levels of organization. For instance, its basic idea of acceleration has shaped the line of declamation and structure of movement, among others.

Within a Noh play, Zeami expanded the model from three to five sections by nesting the Jo-ha-kyū within the Ha, the tripartite’s longest section. Thus, his adaptation can be summarized as: Jo, Ha-jo, Ha-ha, Ha-kyū, Kyū.

This construction is typically found in the dramas from the God's category such as Takasago and Yumiyawata, which were written by Zeami himself. However, this model was an ideal that has proven itself too limiting as the structure for all the different plays. Zeami himself writes that some warrior play could be expanded beyond the normal five sections. The actual composition of a noh drama was dictated more by the dramatic content than the 5 sections based on the jo-ha-kyū.

Dan

Zeami’s formal analysis through the jo-ha-kyū principle established that a noh drama consists of sections referred to in noh terminology as dan. Inspired by analyses of 20th century scholars as found in Frank Hoff & Willi Flindt, “The Life Structure of Noh,” Concerned Theatre Japan, Vol. 2, No. 34, 1973, we have adopted an analyticial structure of two acts, each one divided into five dan, according to the following sequence of actions:

Act 1
Waki Enters
Shite Enters
Dialogue
Shite Performs
Shite Exits

Act 2
Waki Waits
Shite Re-enters
Dialogue
Shite Performs
Shite Exits

A nakairi is often performed between the two acts. Usually, an ai-kyōgen enters at that time and engages with the waki. Acting as a local who is well informed about the place’s history, he introduces information that complements and connects the first and second acts. Performed by a kyōgen actor, the section is seen as external to noh and is not categorized within its stuctural terms like dan or shōdan.

Shōdan

Each dan represents a fixed combination from one to several modules called shōdan. The shōdan are standardized and are found in various plays, where they serve similar structural and dramatic functions and characteristics. Some of them also have a syntactical order where particular one typically follows another. There are about one hundred different types of shōdan that can be categorized as four types: Spoken, Chanted, Entrance and Exit Music, and Dance Music. For more information about individual shōdan and video examples please refer to the Catalog of Shōdan.

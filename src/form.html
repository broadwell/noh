Form

Zeami (c.1363- c.1443) is credited with having perfected Noh as it exists today. When it came to form, he adapted the Gagaku concept of modulation and movement called Jo-ha-kyū. The tripartite can be summarized as: Jo - slow, Ha - development / acceleration, and kyū - fast conclusion, suggesting that most efforts should start slowly, speed up, before rapidly ending. Zeami expanded the model from three to five sections by incorporating the Jo-ha-kyū concept into the Ha, the tripartite’s longest section. Thus, his adaptation can be summarized as: Jo, Ha-jo, Ha-ha, Ha-kyū, Kyū.

Eventually, the principle of Jo-ha-kyū was applied to various levels of organization. For instance, its basic idea of acceleration has shaped declamation and movements, among others.

Dan

It is the modern scholar, Mario Yokomichi (1916-2012) who created a framework to understand the form of Noh play. Referencing Zeami’s five-section concept, he analyzed the form of typical two acts’ Noh plays attributed to Zeami. Then, he has formulated that each act consists of five sections called dan that represent common events in the dramatic development of a Noh play. These are:

First act (mae ba): Waki Enters, Shite Enters, Dialogue, Shite Performs, and Shite Exits
Second act (nochi ba): Waki Waits, Shite Re-enters, Dialogue, Shite Performs, and Shite Exits

It is important to know that the above categorizations agree with the traditional divisions shared by actors and musicians. However, we need to acknowledge that the Zeami/Yokomichi’s ideal model is meant to help the understanding of an act and facilitate comparisons between plays. It is natural that some acts in some Noh plays would lacks some of the dan.

Shōdan

A dan consists of shōdan, which are conventional units that have their own position and syntactical role within a dan. The word shōdan, meaning ‘small dan’ was coined by Mario Yokomichi to further explain the hierarchical character of the form of a Noh play. There are about one hundred different types of shōdan that can be categorized into four types: Spoken, Chanted, Entrance and Exit music, and Dance music. For more information and video examples please refer to the Catalog of Shōdan.

The Form of Hashitomi and Kokaji

The Zeami/Yokomichi model should be viewed as a reference rather than an absolute, since few plays follow it thoroughly. Hashitomi and Kokaji’s form clearly underlines this point, since in their own way they both somewhat deviate from his model.

In the following two figures, the section's name is linked to the beginning of that section within the play, while the shōdan are linked to their respective intermedia analysis.

Not shown in the model but present in both plays is the kyōgen interlude also called the nakairi. It is often performed between the two acts but there are no shōdan associated with it. Usually, a kyōgen actor enters at that moment and engages with the waki. Acting as a local who is well informed about the place’s history, he introduces information that connects the first and second acts.

Catalog of Shōdan: Dance

Jo no mai

Percussion
Strict
Nohkan
Congruent

This refined and calm dance is usually presented by a shite impersonating an old man or a lady, as it is the case with Hashitomi. The complete dance includes five sections, while the abbreviated version has three. The version performed in the play Hashitomi, as it appears on this website, includes all five.

The jo no mai has a three-layered structure articulated by the nohkan, percussion instruments and the choreography of the dance. The nohkan provides a cyclical melodic background, and through its recurring patterns it articulates the dance’s five parts: jo, kakari, and three sections.

The beginning of Hashitomi's jo no mai is preceded by a one-honji bridge from the preceding shōdan, the kuse. The non-congruent jiutai, accompanied by the two percussion instruments in flexible setting, introduces the first line of the poem Genji wrote in response to Lady Yūgao’s poem. The jiutai extends the poem’s last vowel over the next honji overlapping with the beginning of the jo no mai.

The jo is spread over three honji. It is the only section of the dance that is non-congruent.

Kakari can be translated as ‘introduction’, in the case of the jo no mai it refers to the nohkan introducing the dance’s core sequence of patterns called Ji. It is composed of four eight-beat patterns, each one beginning on a second beat and a half of its corresponding honji. They are respectively labelled: Chū, Kan, Kan no Chū, and Ryo, where Kan no Chū is either a variation or repetition of Chū. The melodic shape produced by their sequencing is a four-honji long sinus wave with the Chū pattern in medium range, Kan in the higher range, then back to the medium range with the Kan no Chū, before reaching the lower range with the Ryo pattern. The Ji pattern is played four times in this kakari.

Each of the three sections that follows is composed of two parts. Part-I introduces a phrase composed of a short sequence of a mixture of new and recurring nohkan patterns. For instance, Part-I of the First section introduces a sequence of five patterns: Dan, Kan no Chū, Oroshi, Chū, and Ryo. The structure of Part-I of the Second section is almost identical except that the Oroshi is preceded by a Ryo: Dan, Kan no Chū, Ryo, Oroshi, Kan no Chū, and Ryo. They are all eight-beat long except for the Oroshi which lasts sixteen beats. The structure of the Third section’s Part-I is slightly different, its sequence of patterns being: Dan, Kan no Chū, Ryo, where all patterns are eight-beat long except for the Dan which lasts sixteen beats.

All three sections are followed with two Ji plus one or two additional patterns. The structure of the jo no mai as performed by the Kongō school can be summarized as:

Kakari
First section
Second section
Third section
3 honji
4 Ji
Part-I
Part-I
Part-I
Part-II (2Ji + Chū)
Part-II (2Ji + Chū & Kan)
Part-II (2Ji + Tome)

The instrumental version of the jo no mai included in this Catalog of shōdan starts with the Bridge from the kuse, followed by the jo's three honji, labelled Intro: 1, 2, 3, respectively, which leads to four presentations of the Ji pattern.

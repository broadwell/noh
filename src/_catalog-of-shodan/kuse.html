The name kuse comes from a larger formal notion of kuse-mai, or ‘kuse-dance’. The kuse-mai refers to a sequence of shōdan, the last of which is the kuse proper. It is assumed that the kuse-mai existed before Noh and was abbridged to become its part. A typical kuse-mai consists of six shōdan: shidai, issei, short dance such as an iroe mai, kuri, sashi and kuse with 2 solos lines sung by the shite. Kakitsubata is an example of a play with a typical full kuse-mai but majority of plays present variations of this model featuring just subsets of the shōdan. The most common sequence, when in the first act, consists of kuri, sashi, and kuse with single solo line of the shite. This is the case of Kokaji except for having three shite solo lines. Hashitomi’s kuse is the most abridged as it is a stand-alone kuse with one solo line by the shite.

Kuse can be part of either act, which then may change some of its characteristicsis but it is always one of the most important shōdan of a play. There are several reasons for this status. It is among the most extended of the shōdan, it focuses on the main actor and most importantly, it plays a key role in giving depth to the story or the main character and as such, to the play's formal development.

The standard play starts with the waki arriving on stage and introducing himself. Then, the shite enters and sings a song that reveals nothing about his/her true identity but sparks enough of waki's curiosity for a dialogue between them to follow. The waki questions the shite trying to better understand who he is talking to. The kuse typically describes events related to the shite's story or the context for it. As such it is an indirect but rich poetical answer to the waki's questions.

In the kuse the shite may remain seated (iguse) or may dance. Since the energy and emotional engagement are higher in the second act the danced kuse are more prevalent there. In both cases, the shite only starts dancing after having delivered his last solo line called ageha. Kuse is often the first dance-to-text shōdan of a play,

The kuse is a long thematic chant. Its poetic text potentially in regular rhythm, but most often irregular is set in hiranori. The kuse begins slowly in the lower range and finishes in the higher range, while its overall rhythmic structure moves from unstable to steady. This motion is controlled by the patterns of the two hand-drums whose ratio of mitsuji to tsuzuke patterns, initially favoring the sporadic mitsuji gradually gives way to the regular tsuzuke pattern. At the same time, the declamatory voice setting mitsuji-utai is gradually replaced with the steady tsuzuke-utai. The metric structure (7+5) of the text synchronized with the percussion contributes to the change in overall expression from prosaic to poetic, shifting the emphasis from verbal to non-verbal.

Kuse is sung by the jiutai with the exception of a one or two solos (ageha), that are sung by the shite. The only ageha in Hashitomi is immediately followed by the nohkan’s takane mi kusari pattern, its single entrance in the shōdan. As for the three verses in Kokaji, having performed its naka no takane pattern early at the beginning of the shōdan, the nohkan follows the shite’s first ageha with the takane mi kusari pattern and marks the end of the second one with hishigi takane mi kusari. The shite’s third verse is not followed by a nohkan’s pattern, rather it pauses to begin afresh at the beginning of the dance.

Hashitomi’s kuse appears in the second act. It is sung in yowagin style by the jiutai and shite, who only sings one verse. It is accompanied by the two hand-percussion instruments and the nohkan.

Catalog of Shōdan: Dance

Jonomai

Percussion
Strict
Nohkan
Congruent

This refined and calm dance is usually presented by a shite impersonating a woman, as is the case with Hashitomi, or rarely an old man. It has a three-layered texture: the choreography of the dance, the nohkan and the percussion instruments. The nohkan provides a cyclical melodic background, and through its recurring patterns articulates the dance’s five sections: jo, kakari and three dan sections named after their starting segment called dan. The complete dance, as shown in the full play recording on this website, includes all of the above five sections. Some plays following the jo and kakari, include only one dan.

The beginning of Hashitomi's jonomai is preceded by a one-honji bridge from the preceding shōdan, the kuse. The non-congruent jiutai, accompanied by the two percussion instruments in flexible setting, introduces the first line of the poem Genji wrote in response to Lady Yūgao’s. The jiutai extends the poem’s last vowel over the next honji overlapping with the beginning of the jonomai.

The name jonomai stems from its introductory section called jo, which can be translated as an introduction. The three honji-long section stands apart from the rest of the dance as well as from other instrumental dances as the only section that includes a non-congruent nohkan and percussion in flexible rhythm.

Kakari is the noun derived from the verb kakaru, which means to initiate or to get into something. In the context of the jonomai it could be understood as getting into the congruent-mode phase of the dance, thus being its second introduction after jo.

It is in the kakari that the nohkan introduces the dance’s core sequence of patterns called Ji or 'ground'. It is composed of four eight-beat segments, each one beginning on a second-and-a-half beat of its corresponding honji. They are respectively labelled: Chū, Kan, Kannochū, and Ryo, where Kannochū is either a variation or repetition of Chū. The outline melodic shape produced by their sequencing is a four-honji long wave with the Chū pattern in medium range, Kan in medium but reaching into higher range, then back to the medium range with the Kannochū or Chū, before descending into the lower range in the middle of Ryo. The Ji pattern is played four times in this kakari.

Each of the three dan that follows includes two parts. Part-I can be recognized by an extended 'iya' kakegoe call of the ōtsuzumi preceding every dan. It introduces a short sequence of new and recurring nohkan segments. For instance, Part-I of the First section introduces a sequence of five segments: Dan, Kannochū, Oroshi, Kannochū, and Ryo. Part-I of the Second section is almost identical except that the Oroshi is preceded by a Ryo. This results in the sequence: Dan, Kannochū, Ryo, Oroshi, Kannochū, and Ryo. They are all eight-beat long except for the Oroshi which lasts sixteen beats. Oroshi is also a unique moment of significant slow down. The Third section’s Part-I is slightly different, its sequence of segments being: Dan, Chū, Ryo, where all segments are eight-beat long except for the Dan, which lasts sixteen.

All Part-II sections are comprised of two Ji as heard in kakari, plus unique combinations of one or two additional segments.

The structure of the jonomai as performed by the Kongō school can be summarized as:

Jo
Kakari
First dan
Second dan
Third dan
3 * honji
4 *Ji (Chū, Kan, Kannochū, Ryō)
Dan, Kannochū, Oroshi, Kannochū, Ryō
Dan, Kannochū, Ryō, Oroshi, Kannochū, Ryō
Dan, Kannochū, Ryō
2Ji + Chū
2Ji + Chū, Kan
2Ji + Tome

A special note should be taken of the oroshi pattern. It is not only the longest but it is also the slowest since oroshi means to slow down. The figure shows that the five sections are merged into an overall accelerando that is relaxed in the first and second dan, where the highest peaks correspond to the two-honji long Oroshi.

The instrumental version of the jonomai included in this Catalog of shōdan is abridged. It starts with the bridge from the kuse, followed by the Jo's three honji, labelled Intro: 1, 2, 3, respectively, which leads to Kakari with four presentations of the Ji pattern.

Plot
Akin to a Buddhist monk who recites a prayer on a monotone accompanied by a steady pulse played on the mokugyo, Munechika kneeling on the dais, is accompanied by a rhythmic obliggato as he is chanting blessings on a monotone.
Text
Set in sashinori, Munechika’s prayer acknowledges his talented craft and imperial commission as blessings.
Music
The notto is divided up into two parts: notto-music, followed by the notto-chant. The kotsuzumi and ōtsuzumi’s parts in strict rhythmic setting are joined by a non-congruent nohkan part in the former, and support a non-congruent chant in the latter. The kotsuzumi's staedy pattern is one of the notto's features.
Visuals
The waki walks back on stage and sits in front of the hayashi as it performs the notto-music. As it is about to start the notto-chant, he walks to front-stage where a tsukurimono representing his forge is set. After mounting it, he kneels and picks up a sacred wand.

During the notto-music, Munechika is kneeling on Square 8, facing backward. About half-way in, he stands up and walks towards the dais, steps on it and kneels. Picking up a sacred wand with his right hand, he performs a purifying ritual as he regains faith in his craftmanship. The text of the notto-chant, set in sashinori, includes sixteen lines.

Although the chant and its rhythmic accompaniment are not synchronized through the beats, they are nonetheless connected. Three observations can be made about the interaction between the text and the percussion patterns:

1- While the kotsuzumi is performing its periodic notto uchi tsume pattern characterized by a stroke of each of the honji’s eight beats, the ōtsuzumi player uses his kakegoe to control rallentandi leading to the honji’s third beat where he performs a chon stroke.
For the most part, a complete line of text fits within the eight beats that separate two consecutive chon strokes, as it is the case for the notto’s first three lines. Indeed, having completed a sentence with a sustained-tone, the waki uses the next chon stroke as a reference for positioning his next line, which often starts around the honji's fifth beat.
Longer lines that do not fit within the eight beats span between two successive chon strokes are spread over three or four consecutive chon strokes. In this case, there is no rallentando leading to the intermediate chon stroke(s). Moreover, during in intermediate honji the ōtsuzumi performs a sequence of four, five, or six chon strokes that maintains the momentum.

2- For the most part, the syllables between two chon strokes are spread more or less evenly before leading to a sustained-tone. Sometimes though, the rhythmic distribution of the syllables is uneven, mixing faster and slower durations, where the latter help bring to focus certain keywords. For instance, humility is a highly praise virtu in Japan, as illustrated by Munechika who acknowledges that his craft came to him through “no effort of my own”. This is highlighted in the corresponding line with a rhythmic slowdown on ‘chikara ni arazu’ from the sentence: “Kore watakushi no chikara ni arazu”.

3 - The performance of the fourth line provides an example of how pitch can be combined to rhythm to also emphasize keywords. It is at the conclusion of the fourth line that Munechika announces forcefully, as he is raising the Sacred wand, that he is making his petition, in other words that he is calling for divine assistance for the completion his task. This line lasts three honji and its syllables have been spread as to create a rallentando on the words: “...I make my petition”. The stress on the keyword ‘sasage’ from the line “shi-hō ni honzon no kake-tatematsuri heihaku wo sasage” is further emphasized by Munechika interrupting his repeated tone with a change of pitch followed by a descending glissando that produces a forceful tone, heard as an expression of commitment towards the fulfillment of the imperial request.

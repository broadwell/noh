Plot
The waki-tsure, an Imperial envoy, enters the stage .
Text
Monologue presented by the waki-tsure with factual text set in kotoba.
Music
Unaccompanied monologue.
Visuals
The bridge and stage, more specifically Square 1 represents the Imperial Palace where the waki-tsure is employed.

Entrance music is usually performed for the entrance of a priest, goddess, deity, dragon, and the like. The waki-tsure being an Emperor envoy, he enters the stage without any accompaniment .

After arriving at Square 1, the humblest and quietest stage position, the waki-sture delivers the nanori. It is composed of six lines delivered in kotoba. The text is factual, we learn that he is at the service of Emperor Ichijo, who had a dream oracle suggesting that he gets a blade forged by the swordsmith Munechika, whose house he is now heading to.

The waki-tsure concludes the nanori by bringing his hands together , holding a fan in his right one . While the origin of the gesture is believed to be related to prayer, it has become a standard closing gesture used by secular and religious characters alike, such as the monk in Hashitomi.

Form
First Act - Shite Performs:
Kuri → Sashi → Kuse
Synopsis
In an attempt to encourage Munechika, the jiutai tells the story of two great Chinese Emperors and their famous sword.
Text
Arhythmic poetry.
Music
Chant of the jiutai in tsuyogin style of a text set in hiranori, is accompanied by percussion in strict and the nohkan in non-congruent rhythmic settings. This is the first use of tsuyogin style of singing in the play.
Visuals
The shite walks toward the center Square 9 to kneel together with the waki who remains on Square 6. For the final line of text they turn toward each other after which, the shite turns toward the audience.

The kuri marks an important turning point in the story, as Munechika is taken through a confidence building process, as seen in the text which celebrates the power of two Chinese Emperors and their glorious sword. The non-congruent chant is delivered by the jiutai who speaks for the young boy. It starts with a reference to Liu Bang (256 – 195 BC), the founding Emperor of the Han dynasty. It is said that shortly before dying he exclaimed that “wielding a three-foot sword, as a commoner I conquered the world.” It is followed by a reference to Yang Jian (541 – 604 AD) the first Emperor of the Sui Dynasty and his sword Kei who defeated the House of Chou.

The kuri’s fast and upbeat music is well suited to set this new tone. Moreover, the light singing mode yowagin is from this point on replaced with the heavier tsuyogin, which is commonly used to express bravery and excitement.

The point of intermedia interest of this shōdan focuses on how space and kata can convey meaning despite being abstract components.

At the very beginning of the shōdan, the young boy moves from Square 1 to center stage via Square 8, where he kneels facing front. This act of shortening the physical stage distance between him and Munechika symbolizes that the psychological divide between them is dwindling.

Then, shortly after the introduction of the nohkan’s yuri pattern, the young boy slides left making eye contact with Munechika, underlining the fact that the jiutai is speaking for the former and that this allocution is indeed addressed to the latter. As the shōdan is ending, the young boy slides back to face front as he prepares to engage the audience at the beginning of the next shōdan.

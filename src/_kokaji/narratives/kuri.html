Synopsis
The boy tells the story about two great Chinese Emperors and their famous swords.
Text
Arhythmic poetry.
Music
Chant of the jiutai in tsuyogin style of a text set in hiranori, is accompanied by percussion in strict and the nohkan in non-congruent rhythmic settings. This is the first use of tsuyogin style of singing in the play.
Visuals
The shite walks toward the center Square 9 to kneel together with the waki who remains on Square 6. For the final line of text they turn toward each other after which, the shite turns toward the audience.

WHERE DOES THIS GO???It starts with a reference to Liu Bang (256 – 195 BC), the founding Emperor of the Han dynasty. It is said that shortly before dying he exclaimed that “wielding a three-foot sword, as a commoner I conquered the world.” It is followed by a reference to Yang Jian (541 – 604 AD) the first Emperor of the Sui Dynasty and his sword Kei who defeated the House of Chou.

The Intermedia Index dips down as the chant in non-congruent setting is performed by the jiutai with both actors kneeling statically throughout and only turning toward each other for the concluding line.

However, the kuri’s fast and upbeat music featuring the percussion and the nohkan is well suited to set a new tone for the recount of imperial triumphs. Appropriately, the light singing mode yowagin is from this point on replaced with the heavier tsuyogin, which is commonly used to express bravery and excitement.

When at the beginning of the shōdan, the young boy moves from Square 1 to center stage, the closer proximity of the two characters and their synchronized kneeling down can imply a building of trust. The feeling is reinforced at the end when they turn to face each other.

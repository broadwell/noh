Plot
The jiutai speaks for the shite and tells the story of two great Chinese Emperors and their famous sword.
Text
The story-like text is non-congruent.
Music
The kotsuzumi and ōtsuzumi’s parts in strict rhythmic setting are joined by a non-congruent nohkan part. The beginning of the shōdan is clearly marked by sudden change in tempo and register. The kuri also marks the switching point in the singing mode from yowagin to tsuyogin.
Visuals
The shite and waki are kneeling respectively on Squares 9 and 6.

The kuri marks an important turning point in the story, as Munechika is taken on a confidence building process, as portrayed by the text which celebrates the power of two Chinese Emperors and their glorious sword. The non-congruent chant is delivered by the jiutai who speaks for the young boy. It starts with a reference to Liu Bang (256 – 195 BC), the founding Emperor of the Han dynasty. It is said that shortly before dying he exclaimed that “wielding a three-foot sword, as a commoner I conquered the world.” It is followed by a reference to Yang Jian (541 – 604 AD) the first Emperor of the Sui Dynasty and his sword Kei who defeated the House of Chou.

The kuri’s fast and upbeat feel is well suited to set this new tone. Moreover, the light singing mode yowagin is from this point on replaced with the heavier tsuyogin, which is commonly used to express bravery and excitement.

The point of intermedia interest of this shōdan focuses on how space and kata, besides being abstract components can convey meaning.

At the very beginning of the shōdan, the young boy moves from Square 1 to center stage via Square 8, where he kneels facing front. This act of shortening the physical stage distance between him and Munechika symbolizes that the psychological divide between them is dwindling.

Then, shortly after the introduction of the nohkan’s yuri pattern, the young boy slides left making eye contact with Munechika, underlining the fact that the jiutai is speaking for the former and that this allocution is indeed addressed to latter. As the shōdan is ending, the young boy slides back to face front as he prepares to engage the audience at the beginning of the next shōdan.

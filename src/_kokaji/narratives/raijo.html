Plot
The shite and waki’s exit from the stage, then the kyōgen’s enters.
Text
None.
Music
Taiko, kotsuzumi and ōtsuzumi, and nohkan. Uninterrupted transition from non-congruent music for the shite and waki’s exit to congruent for the kyōgen’s entrance.
Visuals
After the shite and waki have exited the stage, the kyōgen enters.

In clear contrast with the preceding ageuta, the raijo is non-congruent during the exit of the two protagonists, but congruent during the kyōgen’s entrance. The shite is the first one to exit is followed by the waki who does not enter the bridge until the shite has exited. Moreover, the kyōgen does not enter the stage until the waki’s exit has been concluded by a closed curtain.

The featured instrument in the raijo is the nohkan, accompanied by the three percussion instruments whose respective rhythmic patterns merge into a single macro-rhythmic pattern. Since it is not possible to predict how long the exit and entrance will last, musicians follow the mihakarai principle. This is a concept of flexibility that allows the number of repetitions to be determined by the action on stage. In this case, it is determined by the time the actors need to exit and enter the stage. The taiko player who sits in front of the bridge, uses his kakegoe to communicate the changes to the other musicians.

At the beginning of the raijo, the shite takes three special footsteps, each one performed solemnly, separated from the next one by eight beats. Each step symbolizes the shite traveling a long distance at a very fast speed. After the third step, the shite walks out in standard fashion.

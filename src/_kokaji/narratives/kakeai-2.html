Plot
The action of the kakeai-2 is a continuation of the noriji-3, as the deity and Munechika are completing forging of the imperial sword.
Text
Dialogue in kotoba between the two protagonists that esentially narrates the action happening on stage.
Music
The reduction process in the instrumentation is carried one, this time it is the taiko and jiutai that are excluded. The remaining kotsuzumi and ōtsuzumi’s parts is set in flexible rhythm.
Visuals
The use of stage-space is limited since the two protagonists remain on the dais located on Square 4, where they are completing their work.

Musically, the kakeai-2 is like a respite before the storm. Thus, for the span of three honji, the process of instrumental reduction is carried on with the exit of the taiko and the change in the singing body from one singing voice and jiutai to two spoken voices. Also, the kakeai momentarily breaks the honji’s immutability and the synchronicity between beats and syllables.

Story-wise, the kakeai-2 is very much a continuation of the noriji-3, in fact even the synchronization between text and kata is carried on. Thus, we hear and see the two performers completing their work by engraving their respective signature on the blade . The deity’s use of ‘ Little Fox ’ as his signature comes from the fact that foxes, often regarded as messengers are associated with various Inari shrines.

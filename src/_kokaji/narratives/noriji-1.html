Form
Second Act - Waki Waits:
Notto → Noriji-1
Synopsis
Munechika performs a purifying ritual and prays for divine help in carrying out the imperial order.
Text
Poetry in irregular rhythm.
Music
The jiutai sings in tsuyogin style a text set in ōnori with the accompaniment of all three percussion instruments in strict rhythmic setting.
Visuals
The waki is kneeling on the dais at Square 4 and performs purifying gestures with a wand.

The Intermedia Index rises as the music of the noriji 1 becomes congruent with the poetic text set in ōnori and the accompaniment of the three percussion instruments in strict rhythmic setting. The intermedia interest of the noriji 1 lies in how it fits with the adjacent shōdan.

In preparation for the delivery of a prayer, the noriji-1 is visually static. Munechika kneeling on the dais waves a sacred wand as the jiutai speaking for him, calls for divine help. On the other hand, the music builds momentum with its faster tempo and larger instrumentation. The chant is now sung by the jiutai compared to a single voice in the preceding notto and the taiko returns with its first entrance in Act II.

The noriji 1 leads to the hayafue, the entrance music that will bring on stage the second-act shite personifying a divine being. The advent of a deity is a miracle and a climax that is artfully produced by using text and tempo. Set in ōnori, the poetic text counts in average eight syllables per honji. In preparation for the swordsmith’s statement of entreaty “hear and grant my prayer”, the tempo is substantially slowed down by the taiko pattern switching from two to one stroke per beat. This leads to the dramatic line “kinjo-saihai”, which means a prayer. At this point the text setting switches to non-congruent and slows down with only three syllables per honji. The waki sings the first word, the jiutai the second. Against the slow-down in the vocal line, the percussion instruments’ create an accelerando that sets the tone for the dynamic hayafue and the entrance of the second-act shite.

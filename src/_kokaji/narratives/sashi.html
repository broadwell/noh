Plot
Sung exchange between the jiutai and shite, yet the jiutai speaks the shite’s words. Two performing bodies, one mind.
Text
The non-congruent text refers to another glorious Chinese sword.
Music
The kotsuzumi and ōtsuzumi’s parts in flexible rhythmic setting are joined by a non-congruent nohkan part.
Visuals
Shite and waki are kneeling on Squares 9 and 6, respectively.

The sashi’s overall texture is quite complex since the chant and nohkan parts are non-congruent, accompanied by the two-hand percussion set in flexible rhythm. In this context, it is the percussionists’ responsibility to adapt their sparse mitsuji patterns to the chant whose tempo is in constant flux.

The text focuses exclusively on Emperor Huan Tsung (713 – 742 AD) who dreamed about his late Minister Chung K’ei coming to his defense with a sword.
Although the young boy and jiutai alternate in singing, this is not a true dialogue since the jiutai sings the boy’s words lecturing Munechika.

The point of intermedia interest of this shōdan focuses on how music and kata can reinforce the text.

The young boy’s second statement , the slowest of all announces the end of his lecture, leaving it to the jiutai to deliver his conclusive words about the swords: “and their miraculous power is beyond expression!”. The importance of this last statement is furthermore reinforced by two coincidental actions: the young boy engaging Munechika by sliding left to face him, just as the nohkan introduces its kuse no ryō pattern.

Having made his case, the boy slides back to face the audience at the conclusion of the sashi.

Plot
The deity and Munechika are both kneeling on the dais forging together the imperial sword.
Text
Sequence of lines in average of eight syllables per honji set in ōnori sung by jiutai and shite in tsuyogin mode. The text is rather mundane, being a description of the action on stage.
Music
Beginning of a reduction process in the instrumentation. The kotsuzumi, ōtsuzumi, and taiko’s parts are set strict setting, but this shōdan does not include a nohkan's part.
Visuals
This is a descriptive shōdan. The jiutai acts as a narrator describing the action the audience is witnessing. Compared to a dance-to-text, this could be called a movements-to-text. Interestingly enough, the shōdan includes also some sound-to-text (onomatopea).

The point of intermedia interest of this shōdan focuses on movements that imitate the text, and text that imitates the movements with onomatopea.

Conversely to the other two noriji this one is scored for two voices, the shite and jiutai, but this is not a dialogue, rather they are both acting as narrators, taking turns in describing the action. Text and kata are in perfect synchronicity in this noriji, where what is said is what is seen.

The noriji-3 is divided up into segments, each one clearly articulated by the taiko’s rhythmic cycles, the beginning of each one identifiable by a head pattern kashira:

1- We are told that the deity is a young boy who ascends the forge mound before bowing to the waki. This formal introduction concludes the noriji’s introduction, in phase with the first taiko rhythmic cycle.

2- During the next segment, the deity asked Munechika to bring the metal to be forged into the blade, and he obliges. Then, the importance of the first three hammer strokes is underlined by the characters imitating the sound of the hammers on the blade with the onomatopea: ‘chō’ .

3- Finally, the text highlights the magnificence of the event. As we hear that the strokes are heard through heaven and earth, the shite jumps out of the dais covers his head with his sleeves to indicate that he is no more physically present in this location. His Up-Left-Right-Down Head motion suggests that he can hear the sound of the strokes bouncing off through space. This is followed by the waki’s Left-Right Head motion which confirms that he can also hear it .

Form
First Act - Shite Exits:
Kakeai-1 → Ageuta-3 → Raijo
Synopsis
The young boy announces that after changing into a supernatural form, he will return to help Munechika and then, vanishes into the evening clouds of Mount Inari.
Text
Poetry in irregular rhythm.
Music
The text set in hiranori is sung by the jiutai in tsuyogin mode. The accompanying percussion is in strict rhythmic setting.
Visuals
In the first part the shite and the waki remain kneeling on Squares 9 and 6, respectively. The shite stands up and the waki bows to him. Then, the shite performs a brief dance on the stage’s right lateral and ends on Square 1.

The shōdan is a high Intermedia Index point of the Shite Exits dan. The congruent setting of jiutai chant accompanied by percussion in strict rhythmic setting give weight to the promise made by the deity. The brief energetic sequence of kata in the second half of the shōdan testify to its strength.

All the kata performed in the shōdan are structural but the text and context provide them with additional meaning. The first sentence of an ageuta is typically repeated. In this case, the jiutai who speaks for the young boy, sings twice the following line:“I will change into supernatural form.”, but the first time, the boy kneeling center-stage faces front, hence addressing the audience, but for the repetition, he slides left to engage Munechika.

Context provides the next three standard kata with a ceremonial tone. As the jiutai still speaking for the young boy tells that he will come back and join forces with the swordsmith, the boy stands up and faces Munechika, as he performs a Forward Pointing followed by an Open-retreat, two kata that visually reinforce his commitment. The swordsmith replies by ceremoniously bowing. In doing so, he confirms for the first time in the drama, that he now understands and accepts their hierarchical relationship: the helping deity, and the helped swordsmith.

Then, as the jiutai refers to the clouds of Mount Inari, the tempo suddenly picks up. In preparation for his imminent departure into the Inari sky, the boy builds tension by moving to the very dynamic Square 3, where he circles and faces the waki. Then he releases the tension by going to Square 1 where he performs a Circlet point, to symbolize his disappearance, which is confirmed by Munechika, who slides right to face front and points forward where we deduce the boy just vanished. For the audience, the boy performs an Open-retreat as a closing kata before starting his exit.

When this ageuta is not followed by the exit music raijo, the nohkan player concludes it with a Takane pattern. But when followed by the exit/entrance music raijo as it is the case in this version, the Takane is replaced with the Hishigi pattern, whose beginning overlaps with the end of the ageuta and the beginning of the raijo.

The syllabic structure is (5+5, 5+6, 4+6, 6+6, 7+6, 5+5, 7+5, 7+5). Like the kuse and kiri, it is when narrative becomes more important than poetry, that the text moves from regular to irregular rhythm. While the text of most ageuta is regular, Kokaji's Ageuta 3 narrates the dramatic departure of the diety, thus its irregular rhythm.

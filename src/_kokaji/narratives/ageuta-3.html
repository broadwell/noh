Form
First Act - Shite Exits:
Kakeai-1 → Ageuta-3 → Raijo
Synopsis
The young boy announces that after changing into a supernatural form, he will return to help Munechika and then, vanishes into the evening clouds of Mount Inari.
Text
Poetry in irregular rhythm.
Music
The text set in hiranori is sung by the jiutai in tsuyogin mode. The accompanying percussion is in strict rhythmic setting.
Visuals
In the first part the shite and the waki remain kneeling on Squares 9 and 6, respectively. The shite stands up and the waki bows to him. Then, the shite performs a brief dance on the stage’s right lateral and ends on Square 1.

The shōdan is a highest Intermedia Index point of the Shite Exits dan. The congruent setting of jiutai chant accompanied by percussion in strict rhythm, and the typical for ageuta repeated first line, give weight to the commitment made by the deity. The significant acceleration and the brief but very energetic sequence of kata in the second half of the shōdan fit well a young boy but speak also to the diety's strength.

All the kata performed in the shōdan are structural but the text and context provide them with additional meaning. As we hear the diety say (chanted by the jiutai) that "I will come without fail, at the appointed time"Munechika the boy stands up and facing Munechika performs a Forward Pointing followed by an Open-retreat, two standard kata that here visually reinforce his commitment. The swordsmith replies by ceremoniously bowing.

Then, as the jiutai refers to the clouds of Mount Inari, the tempo suddenly picks up. In preparation for his imminent departure into the sky, the boy builds tension by moving to the very dynamic Square 3, where he circles and faces the waki. Then he releases the tension by going to Square 1 where he performs a Circlet point. This symbolizes his disappearance, which is confirmed by Munechika, who slides right to face front and points forward where we deduce the boy just vanished.

DO WE NEED THIS? HERE? When this ageuta is not followed by the exit music raijo, the nohkan player concludes it with a Takane pattern. But when followed by the exit/entrance music raijo as it is the case in this version, the Takane is replaced with the Hishigi pattern, whose beginning overlaps with the end of the ageuta and the beginning of the raijo.

Like the kuse and kiri, it is when narrative becomes more important than poetry, that the text moves from regular to irregular rhythm. While the text of most ageuta is regular, Kokaji's Ageuta 3 narrates the dramatic departure of the diety, thus its irregular rhythm (5+5, 5+6, 4+6, 6+6, 7+6, 5+5, 7+5, 7+5).
